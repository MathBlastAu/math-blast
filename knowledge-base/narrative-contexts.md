## How to Use This Library

Each context below is tagged with:
- **Topics:** which of the four math topics it supports
- **Age range:** recommended year bands
- **Story genre:** the type of narrative
- **Hook type:** what creates the engagement (urgency, mystery, fairness, etc.)
- **Gender note:** how to keep it inclusive

Many contexts work across multiple topics — these are especially valuable as **recurring settings** that readers bond with across multiple issues.

## 🌌 SPACE & SPACECRAFT

### 1. Mission Control Countdown
**Topics:** Time, Division, Balancing Equations
**Age range:** Years 2–6 (ages 7–12)
**Story genre:** Adventure, Suspense
**Hook type:** Urgency, countdown, logistics

The crew must coordinate a launch, spacewalk, and docking — all on a tight schedule, with limited fuel divided among missions.
- Time: elapsed time, schedules, 24-hour time
- Division: fuel/crew/supplies divided among missions
- Equations: "Fuel needed = distance ÷ engine efficiency + reserve" — find the missing value

**Why it works cognitively:** Children respond strongly to time pressure and mission-critical stakes. Countdown creates emotional investment.
**Why it works motivationally:** Space travel is aspirational; even young children know about rockets. "Mission control" gives kids an authoritative role.
**Gender note:** Name mission controllers, astronauts, engineers, and scientists using mixed genders and diverse cultural names. Avoid defaulting male for "commander" roles.

### 2. Space Ration Management
**Topics:** Fractions, Division, Balancing Equations
**Age range:** Years 2–6 (ages 7–12)
**Story genre:** Survival, Problem-solving
**Hook type:** Scarcity, fairness

Limited food, water, or oxygen must be divided fairly among crew — or allocated by fraction of daily needs.
- Fractions: "We have ¾ of a tank left. How long will it last if we use 1/8 per day?"
- Division: equal sharing among crew; remainder means someone gets less
- Equations: "Oxygen per person × number of crew = total needed. We have [X]. How many crew can we support?"

**Why it works cognitively:** Resource allocation maps directly to fraction/division structure. Fairness is emotionally salient even for young children.
**Why it works motivationally:** Survival narrative raises stakes naturally.
**Gender note:** Crew members performing logistics, engineering, and science should be mixed gender. The "protector" role (someone ensuring fairness) works for any character.

### 3. Space Station Shift Roster
**Topics:** Time, Division, Balancing Equations
**Age range:** Years 4–6 (ages 9–12)
**Story genre:** Workplace drama, Puzzle
**Hook type:** Scheduling mystery

The station's roster has gaps. Shifts must cover 24 hours, each crew member works equal hours, but someone has double-booked.
- Time: 24-hour time, elapsed time, scheduling
- Division: equal shift hours distributed among crew
- Equations: write and solve equations from schedule constraints

**Why it works cognitively:** Timetable reading is curriculum-specific (Y5); this makes it purposeful.
**Gender note:** Station commander, engineer, scientist, medic — all genders across all roles.

### 4. Planet Gravity Scale
**Topics:** Division, Fractions, Balancing Equations
**Age range:** Years 4–6 (ages 9–12)
**Story genre:** Science adventure
**Hook type:** Real science (NASA-connected)

Objects weigh differently on different planets (Moon = 1/6 Earth, Mars ≈ 3/8 Earth). Characters calculate their weight, design experiments, or compare planet data.
- Fractions: planet gravity as a fraction of Earth's
- Division: "If 84 kg on Earth, what on the Moon?"
- Equations: set up formula-based equations; find unknown planet from given weight

**Why it works cognitively:** Real NASA science; connects math to physics. The "WOW factor" of "I'd weigh 14 kg on the Moon!" is motivating.
**Gender note:** Scientists and explorers of any gender. Include characters from non-Western backgrounds (reference space programs from India, UAE, China, etc.).

### 5. The Alien Trade Market
**Topics:** Fractions, Division, Balancing Equations
**Age range:** Years 3–6 (ages 8–12)
**Story genre:** Fantasy marketplace, Negotiation
**Hook type:** Trade, fairness, puzzle

At an alien bazaar, items are traded by weight, or in fraction-quantities. "I'll trade you ⅔ of my moon dust for ¼ of your crystal stack."
- Fractions: comparing and combining fractions in trade context
- Division: dividing quantities to make the trade fair
- Equations: writing equations for what each trader has before/after

**Why it works cognitively:** Trade and exchange are intuitive; bar models map directly to "what each party has."
**Why it works motivationally:** Fantasy market + negotiation = engaging and playful.
**Gender note:** Traders, merchants, and alien characters are all genders; names from diverse cultural backgrounds or alien names entirely.

## 🔍 MYSTERY & DETECTIVE

### 6. The Space Station Thief
**Topics:** Division, Balancing Equations, Fractions
**Age range:** Years 3–6 (ages 8–12)
**Story genre:** Mystery, Detective
**Hook type:** Investigation, hidden information

Some cargo has gone missing. Clues involve divided quantities, missing numbers in equations, and fractional amounts of items. "There were 48 crystals. Now there are only ⅜ of that left. How many were taken?"
- Fractions: what fraction remains; what fraction was taken
- Division: how could the missing items have been split to cover tracks?
- Equations: "Items remaining = total − stolen; stolen = n. Find n."

**Why it works cognitively:** Deductive reasoning scaffolds mathematical problem-solving. Finding the "solution" mirrors the math answer-finding process.
**Gender note:** Detective can be any gender. Multiple suspects of mixed backgrounds and genders.

---

### 7. The Robot's Broken Code
**Topics:** Balancing Equations, Fractions, Time
**Age range:** Years 2–6 (ages 7–12)
**Story genre:** Tech adventure, Puzzle
**Hook type:** Decoding, fixing

A robot's programming has corrupted numbers replaced with symbols. Students "debug" the code by solving equations. Each solved equation unlocks a new room or capability.
- Equations: solve for unknown values in equations (symbols instead of letters/boxes = novelty)
- Fractions: robot needs exactly ¾ power to open a door; current level + top-up = ¾
- Time: robot's internal clock is broken; use clues to reset it

**Why it works cognitively:** Coding/debugging metaphor is deeply motivating for current cohort of kids.
**Gender note:** Robot designer and technician can be any gender. Robots themselves can have non-gendered names.

---

### 8. The Lost Diary / Time Capsule
**Topics:** Time, Fractions (elapsed fractions of years), Division
**Age range:** Years 4–6 (ages 9–12)
**Story genre:** Historical mystery, Treasure hunt
**Hook type:** Discovery, calendar reasoning

An ancient diary has dates without full context. Students reconstruct the timeline: "The explorer set off 3 weeks after the spring equinox" — when was that? The capsule was buried exactly half a year before the diary's final entry.
- Time: calendar arithmetic, elapsed time, dates
- Fractions: half a year, ¾ of a century, fraction of a month
- Division: "The journey was split into 4 equal legs of 3 days each — how long in total?"

**Why it works cognitively:** Historical context provides intrinsic motivation. Calendar math in a purposeful setting.
**Gender note:** Explorer, scientist, ancestor — any gender. Multiple historical/cultural settings possible (ancient Egypt, Polynesian navigation, Indigenous astronomy).

## 🍕 FOOD & COOKING

### 9. The Galactic Bake-Off
**Topics:** Fractions, Time, Division, Balancing Equations
**Age range:** Years 1–5 (ages 6–10)
**Story genre:** Competition, Cooking
**Hook type:** Competition, real-world context

Teams of space chefs compete. Each dish requires precise fractions of ingredients, different cooking times, and equal serving portions.
- Fractions: ¾ cup of stardust flour; double the recipe (multiply fractions)
- Time: "The cake takes 45 minutes; the sauce 20 minutes — when does each start so they're ready at 3:30pm?"
- Division: "Cut the cake into 8 equal slices for the judges"
- Equations: "The recipe calls for equal amounts of X and Y, totalling 1½ cups. How much of each?"

**Why it works cognitively:** Cooking provides authentic fraction/time/division contexts. Multiple representations (measuring cups, timers) are concrete.
**Why it works motivationally:** Food is universally appealing; baking shows are popular.
**Gender note:** All genders cook and compete. Avoid making the only female character the baker. Mix up who wins and who helps.

### 10. Alien Food Festival
**Topics:** Fractions, Division
**Age range:** Years 1–4 (ages 5–9)
**Story genre:** Cultural festival, Sharing
**Hook type:** Fairness, novelty

At the Galactic Food Festival, each species brings a dish to share. Portions are divided; some dishes aren't split evenly.
- Fractions: what fraction of the pie does each alien get?
- Division: fair sharing of different quantities

**Why it works cognitively:** Food sharing is the most intuitive division/fraction context for young learners.
**Gender note:** All food roles equal across genders; alien species are beyond gender stereotypes.

## 🏅 SPORT & COMPETITION

### 11. Space Olympics
**Topics:** Fractions, Time, Division
**Age range:** Years 2–6 (ages 7–12)
**Story genre:** Competition, Sport
**Hook type:** Leaderboard, winning, comparison

Competitors from different planets score fractions of points, complete events in specific times, and share prize resources.
- Fractions: "Zara scored 5½ points and Rex scored ¼ more — what's Rex's score?"
- Time: race results in minutes/seconds; elapsed time between events
- Division: prize credits shared among a team

**Why it works cognitively:** Leaderboards are natural ordering/comparison contexts. Sport is intrinsically motivating for many.
**Gender note:** All genders compete in all sports. The leading athlete should be female at least half the time. Include athletes with disability.

---

### 12. Speed and Distance Racing
**Topics:** Division (rates), Time, Fractions
**Age range:** Years 4–6 (ages 9–12)
**Story genre:** Racing, Engineering
**Hook type:** Speed, competition, optimization

A space buggy race across a planet's surface. Students calculate speeds, distances, times, and fuel efficiency — comparing options.
- Division: speed = distance ÷ time; how do they measure up?
- Time: race duration, pit stop timing
- Fractions: "She used ⅔ of the fuel in the first half — how much is left for the second half?"

**Why it works cognitively:** Rate problems (km/h) feel meaningful in a racing context. "Who wins?" is immediately motivating.
**Gender note:** Drivers, mechanics, and designers of any gender.

## 🌿 NATURE & ANIMALS

### 13. The Wildlife Survey
**Topics:** Division, Fractions, Balancing Equations
**Age range:** Years 3–6 (ages 8–12)
**Story genre:** Science, Conservation
**Hook type:** Real-world concern, data analysis

Scientists survey an alien planet's ecosystem. They count species, calculate what fraction of the ecosystem each occupies, and set up equations from population ratios.
- Division: "There are 360 creatures across 9 species — is each species equally represented?"
- Fractions: what fraction of the total is each species?
- Equations: "Predators = ⅕ of prey. Together they total 120. How many predators?"

**Why it works cognitively:** Data and conservation contexts connect to cross-curriculum science.
**Gender note:** Scientists, ecologists, rangers — all genders.

---

### 14. Animal Feeding Schedule
**Topics:** Division, Fractions, Time
**Age range:** Years 1–4 (ages 5–9)
**Story genre:** Caring, Animals
**Hook type:** Daily routine, nurturing

The space zoo's animals need to be fed on a schedule. Rations must be divided correctly, times must be followed, and portions adjusted for each animal's size.
- Fractions: "The large creature gets 1½ times the normal ration"
- Division: sharing feed among multiple animals
- Time: feeding times on a schedule; elapsed time between feeds

**Why it works cognitively:** Nurturing/caretaking appeals broadly. Division and fractions in a "necessary" context.
**Gender note:** Zookeeper, veterinarian, caretaker — any gender. Name characters diversely.

## 🏙️ CITIES & COMMUNITY

### 15. Building the Space Colony
**Topics:** Division, Fractions, Balancing Equations, Time
**Age range:** Years 3–6 (ages 8–12)
**Story genre:** City-building, Architecture
**Hook type:** Design, planning, community

Students plan a small space colony: allocate land (fractions of total area), schedule construction (time), divide resources, and write equations for construction rates.
- Fractions: "The hospital takes ¼ of the grid. The school takes ⅙. How much land is left?"
- Time: "Construction schedule — if each building takes 3 weeks, when will the colony be ready?"
- Division: "48 solar panels divided equally among 6 buildings"
- Equations: "We need 2 workers per building + 4 supervisors total = n workers. n = ?"

**Why it works cognitively:** City-building is intrinsically motivating; every piece of math has a visible outcome.
**Gender note:** Architects, builders, mayors, planners — any gender. Emphasise collaborative decision-making.

## ⏳ TIME TRAVEL

### 16. The Time Traveller's Notebook
**Topics:** Time, Fractions, Division
**Age range:** Years 3–6 (ages 8–12)
**Story genre:** Fantasy, Time travel
**Hook type:** Adventure, paradox

A time traveller jumps through history and must keep track of how long they've been gone (elapsed time), figure out what fraction of a journey has been completed, and divide their energy rations across legs of the trip.
- Time: elapsed time, calendar calculations, time zones
- Fractions: "I'm ¾ of the way through my journey. How much further?"
- Division: energy rations for each time-jump leg

**Why it works cognitively:** Time travel makes abstract time calculations concrete and purposeful.
**Gender note:** Time traveller is any gender. Historical figures they meet are diverse.

## 🎵 MUSIC & RHYTHM

### 17. The Space DJ
**Topics:** Fractions, Balancing Equations
**Age range:** Years 3–5 (ages 8–10)
**Story genre:** Music, Creativity
**Hook type:** Creative challenge, patterns

A DJ must fill exactly 1 bar of music (= 1 whole) using note tiles: ½ notes, ¼ notes, ⅛ notes. Can they fill it exactly?
- Fractions: adding fractions to make exactly 1 whole
- Equations: ½ + ¼ + □ = 1 — what goes in the box?

**Why it works cognitively:** Music note values literally ARE fractions. Perfect concrete model.
**Gender note:** Any character can be a musician/DJ. Music from diverse global traditions can be referenced.

## 🧬 SCIENCE & LAB

### 18. The Chemistry Lab
**Topics:** Fractions, Balancing Equations, Division
**Age range:** Years 4–6 (ages 9–12)
**Story genre:** Sci-fi lab, Experiment
**Hook type:** Precise measurement, discovery

Scientists mix fractions of chemicals to create new materials. Getting the balance wrong has consequences. Equations describe the ratios.
- Fractions: "Mix ⅔ of Chemical A with ⅓ of Chemical B"
- Equations: proportion equations from ratios; solve for missing amount
- Division: scale a recipe up or down by dividing/multiplying quantities

**Why it works cognitively:** Chemistry provides authentic ratio/fraction contexts. The "right answer matters" increases engagement.
**Gender note:** Scientists and lab assistants of any gender; avoid white-coat male stereotype.

## 🎮 GAMES & VIRTUAL WORLDS

### 19. The Points Shop
**Topics:** Balancing Equations, Division, Fractions
**Age range:** Years 2–5 (ages 7–10)
**Story genre:** Video game, Fantasy RPG
**Hook type:** Game mechanics, rewards

In a space RPG, characters earn points and spend them on upgrades. Equations describe costs. Division determines how to split resources in a team.
- Equations: "You need 50 points to unlock Level 5. You have 32 and earn 6 per mission. How many missions?"
- Division: splitting team rewards equally; upgrading proportionally
- Fractions: "She gets ¼ of the team's total points for being MVP"

**Why it works cognitively:** Game contexts have intrinsic internal logic that makes equations feel purposeful.
**Gender note:** All genders are players; avoid gendering specific classes or roles.

## 📡 COMMUNICATION & TECHNOLOGY

### 20. The Signal Delay Puzzle
**Topics:** Time, Division, Fractions
**Age range:** Years 5–6 (ages 10–12)
**Story genre:** Science, Communication
**Hook type:** Real science, puzzle

Radio signals travel at the speed of light. A signal from Mars takes ~20 minutes to reach Earth. Students calculate when to send a message to arrive at the right time.
- Time: elapsed time with a twist (signal delay is subtracted/added)
- Division: if light travels 300,000 km per second, how long to reach Mars at [distance]?
- Fractions: the signal has decayed to ¾ of its original strength — is it still usable?

**Why it works cognitively:** Real NASA/ESA science. Makes abstract time calculations concrete.
**Gender note:** Mission communicators and scientists are diverse.

## Gender Inclusivity Checklist

For every story context in Math Blast:
- [ ] At least one lead character who is female or non-binary
- [ ] STEM roles (engineer, scientist, coder) given to diverse genders
- [ ] Physical/active roles (explorer, athlete, pilot) not defaulting to male
- [ ] Names from diverse cultural backgrounds represented (not all Anglo)
- [ ] Caring/nurturing roles (medic, teacher) not defaulting to female
- [ ] Competition/leadership roles distributed across genders
- [ ] No character's math ability linked to their gender
