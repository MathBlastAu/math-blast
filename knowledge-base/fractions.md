# Fractions — Pedagogy Knowledge Base

*For Math Blast | Australian Curriculum Years 1–6 | Ages 5–12*

---

## 1. Core Pedagogy & Best Practice

### Why Fractions Are Hard
Fractions are the single most researched area of difficulty in primary mathematics. They require students to simultaneously hold multiple sub-concepts:
- Fractions as **part of a whole** (area model)
- Fractions as **part of a collection** (set model)
- Fractions as a **number on a number line** (measure model)
- Fractions as **division** (e.g. 3 ÷ 4 = ¾)
- Fractions as **ratio** (more advanced)

Research (Charalambous & Pitta-Pantazi, 2007; Siegler et al., 2013) shows that **number line competency with fractions in Year 5 is one of the strongest predictors of algebra success in secondary school**. Yet most instruction focuses only on part-whole with area models (pizzas, circles).

### Evidence-Based Teaching Approaches

#### CPA Progression (Concrete → Pictorial → Abstract)
The most well-supported sequence for fractions:

**Concrete:** Physical manipulation — folding paper, cutting fruit/playdough, using fraction tiles, Cuisenaire rods, pattern blocks. Students *feel* equal parts.

**Pictorial:** Bar models (Singapore-style), area diagrams, number lines, strip diagrams. Students *see* the relationship.

**Abstract:** Notation (½, ¾), fraction arithmetic, equivalent fraction rules. Students *represent* symbolically.

**Key rule:** Never rush to the abstract. Many fraction misconceptions stem from introducing notation before conceptual understanding is solid.

#### Mastery Approach
- Equal access: every student achieves conceptual understanding at each step before moving on.
- Variation theory: present the same concept in multiple representations (vary one thing at a time).
- Anchor problems: rich, open problems students return to across multiple lessons.

#### Fraction Talks (Spinney, 2017)
Visual images presented to the class — "What fraction is shaded?" — with multiple valid answers. Builds flexible thinking and challenges the assumption that fractions always look like a pizza slice.

#### Number Line as Core Model
Research strongly supports the number line as the most powerful fraction model because:
- It positions fractions as numbers (not just parts of shapes)
- It reveals magnitude and ordering
- It bridges to decimals and percentages naturally
- It prevents the "bigger denominator = bigger fraction" misconception

**Recommendation:** Introduce number lines early (Year 2) and return to them at every year level.

### Most Effective Instructional Sequence
1. Equal sharing with physical objects (concrete)
2. Naming parts of a whole (halves, quarters, thirds) — area model with paper folding
3. Recognising and making equal parts (critical — many kids skip this)
4. Set/collection models ("half of 8 stickers")
5. Number line placement
6. Equivalent fractions (concrete → pictorial → abstract)
7. Comparing and ordering fractions
8. Addition/subtraction of fractions with like denominators
9. Addition/subtraction with unlike denominators
10. Multiplication and division of fractions

### Best Manipulatives & Visual Models
| Tool | Best For | Year Level |
|------|----------|------------|
| Paper folding | Equal parts, halves, quarters | F–2 |
| Fraction tiles/circles | Part-whole understanding | 1–4 |
| Cuisenaire rods | Equivalent fractions, relative size | 2–5 |
| Pattern blocks | Non-standard wholes, equivalent fractions | 2–4 |
| Bar/strip models | Part-whole, fractions of collections, comparison | 2–6 |
| Number lines | Ordering, equivalence, magnitude | 2–6 |
| Fraction walls | Equivalence, comparison | 3–5 |
| Double number lines | Fractions as ratios, rates | 5–6 |

## 2. Common Misconceptions by Age

### Foundation–Year 1 (Ages 5–6)
**Misconception:** "Halves just means two pieces" — they don't need to be equal.
- *Why:* Children haven't yet developed the concept of equal parts as definitional.
- *Fix:* Always contrast equal vs. unequal splitting. Ask "Is this a fair half?" Bring in fairness as a concept.

**Misconception:** "Half of the group" means "take some away" or "one pile."
- *Why:* Sharing is intuitive but they may not connect it to fraction language.
- *Fix:* Explicit language linking — "When I share equally into 2 groups, each group is ONE HALF."

### Years 2–3 (Ages 7–8)
**Misconception:** Larger denominator = larger fraction (e.g. ⅛ > ½)
- *Why:* Whole-number thinking bleeds over — 8 > 2.
- *Fix:* Number lines, fraction walls. "The more pieces you cut something into, the SMALLER each piece gets."

**Misconception:** ½ always looks like a triangle or semi-circle.
- *Why:* Overexposure to circle/pizza models.
- *Fix:* Fraction Talks — show fractions in rectangles, irregular shapes, sets, number lines.

**Misconception:** ¼ of different wholes is always the same size.
- *Why:* Unit fraction understood as an absolute amount rather than relative.
- *Fix:* "¼ of a big pizza and ¼ of a small pizza — which is more pizza?" Use different-sized wholes consistently.

### Years 4–5 (Ages 9–10)
**Misconception:** You add/subtract numerators AND denominators (⅓ + ½ = 2/5)
- *Why:* Pattern-matching from whole number addition; both "top" and "bottom" seem to be separate numbers to add.
- *Fix:* Return to CPA — use fraction tiles to *show* why this doesn't work. "Can you cut a pizza into thirds and a pizza into halves and call the slices fifths?"

**Misconception:** Equivalent fractions are "different fractions"
- *Why:* Symbolic notation masks the sameness.
- *Fix:* Physically fold paper to show that ½ and 2/4 are literally the same amount. Use number lines.

**Misconception:** Mixed numbers and improper fractions are unrelated
- *Why:* Taught as procedures rather than connected concepts.
- *Fix:* Number lines showing 1½ and 3/2 landing on the same spot.

### Year 6 (Ages 11–12)
**Misconception:** Multiplying fractions makes them bigger (mirroring whole-number multiplication)
- *Why:* "Multiplication makes bigger" is a deeply held belief from Years 1–4.
- *Fix:* "½ × ½ means half OF a half." Estimating before calculating. Area models.

**Misconception:** When dividing fractions, "flip the wrong one" or confuse which fraction to invert.
- *Why:* Procedural memory without conceptual understanding.
- *Fix:* Teach "how many of these fit into that?" as a question first. Use bar models.

**Misconception:** Percentages and fractions are unrelated.
- *Why:* Taught in isolation at different times.
- *Fix:* Consistent number line showing fractions, decimals, percentages as the same points.

## 3. Age-Stage Progression

### Foundation–Year 1 (Ages 5–6)
**Australian Curriculum Content:** Recognise and describe one-half as one of two equal parts of a whole (Year 1 AC9M1N03).

**Cognitive Stage:** Pre-operational to early concrete operational (Piaget). Learning through direct manipulation and physical experience. Strong reliance on what things *look like*.

**Entry Point:** Equal sharing in play — "Let's share these 4 cookies so we both get the same." The language of fairness is the bridge.

**Key Vocabulary:** half, halves, equal parts, share, fair, same, whole
*(Avoid: fraction, numerator, denominator at this stage)*

**Appropriate Problem Types:**
- "Fold this paper into 2 equal parts. Colour half."
- "Share 6 grapes equally between 2 people. How many each?"
- "Is this shape cut in half? How do you know?"

**Story/Narrative Complexity:** Simple, single-character problems. One action, one outcome. Characters should be doing immediately relatable things (eating, sharing, playing). Vocabulary: everyday words only.

---

### Years 2–3 (Ages 7–8)
**Australian Curriculum Content:**
- Y2: Recognise and represent halves, quarters and eighths (AC9M2N03)
- Y3: Represent unit fractions including ½, ¼, ⅓, ⅕ and their multiples to a complete whole (AC9M3N03)

**Cognitive Stage:** Concrete operational. Can classify, seriate, and understand conservation. Beginning to handle two-step thinking with physical support.

**Extending From Foundation:** Move from halves only → quarters, thirds, eighths. Introduce set/collection model alongside area model. Begin number line.

**Key Vocabulary:** quarter, third, fifth, eighth, equal parts, numerator, denominator (introduced gradually), fraction, whole, number line, unit fraction

**Appropriate Problem Types:**
- "Shade ¾ of this rectangle."
- "Put ⅓ on this number line."
- "Which is bigger — ½ or ¼? How do you know?"
- "What fraction of this group of 8 stars are yellow?"

**Story/Narrative Complexity:** Two-character stories, one small challenge to solve. Can handle mild tension (something is missing, someone needs help). Cause-and-effect is clear. 2–3 sentence problem setups work well.

---

### Years 4–5 (Ages 9–10)
**Australian Curriculum Content:**
- Y4: Count by quarters, halves and thirds; locate and represent these fractions on a number line (AC9M4N02). Equivalent fractions (AC9M4N03).
- Y5: Compare and order common unit fractions; recognise equivalence (AC9M5N02). Add and subtract fractions with the same/related denominators (AC9M5N03).

**Cognitive Stage:** Solid concrete operational, emerging formal operational. Can handle multi-step problems, beginning to reason abstractly with support.

**Moving Toward Abstraction:** Bar models become primary tool (less physical). Students reason about equivalent fractions symbolically. Introduce improper fractions and mixed numbers.

**Key Vocabulary:** equivalent, numerator, denominator, improper fraction, mixed number, lowest terms, simplify, common denominator, benchmark fractions (½, ¼, ¾)

**Appropriate Problem Types:**
- "Is ⅔ equivalent to 4/6? Prove it two different ways."
- "Order these fractions on a number line: ¾, ⅓, 5/6, ½"
- "Sam ran ⅗ of a kilometre and Jo ran ⅘. How much further did Jo run?"
- "A recipe needs 1¾ cups of flour. How many quarter-cups is that?"

**Story/Narrative Complexity:** Multi-character stories with a genuine problem to solve (quest, mystery, competition). Students can handle stories where fractions are embedded naturally in a situation. Red herrings work. 4–6 sentence setups. Characters can have different perspectives/answers that both need examination.

---

### Year 6 (Ages 11–12)
**Australian Curriculum Content:**
- Multiply and divide fractions and decimals (AC9M6N04, AC9M6N05)
- Connect fractions, decimals, and percentages (AC9M6N06)
- Apply to problems involving rates and ratios

**Cognitive Stage:** Emerging formal operational. Can handle hypothetical reasoning, variable thinking, proportional reasoning.

**Consolidation & Complexity:** Fractions as operators (⅔ of 450), fractions in context of ratios and rates, connecting to decimals/percentages, fraction arithmetic with unlike denominators.

**Key Vocabulary:** percentage, decimal equivalent, rate, ratio, proportion, simplest form, reciprocal, scale factor

**Appropriate Problem Types:**
- "A spacecraft uses ⅔ of its fuel in the first half of the journey. If it started with 450L, how much is left?"
- "The probability of landing on a meteor is 3/10. Express as a decimal and percentage."
- Multi-step problems mixing fractions and whole numbers.

**Story/Narrative Complexity:** Full story arc with protagonist, obstacle, and resolution. Can handle ambiguity, multiple steps, and non-obvious setups. Humour and irony land. Character-driven problems ("Zara thinks ⅔ is bigger than ¾ — is she right and why?"). Can handle problems where the math explains a real-world mystery.

## 4. Innovative Approaches

### Game-Based Learning
**Fraction Talks** (fractiontalks.com)
Images shown with "What fraction is shaded?" — multiple correct answers. Generates rich discussion. Easily adapted for a magazine page format: show a space station grid, ask what fraction is occupied by each species.

**Fraction War (Card Game)**
Two players draw fraction cards and compare. Simple, fast, self-checking. Builds ordering intuition.

**Fraction Clothesline**
A string is strung across the room; students hang cards at the correct position. Spatial, embodied, social. Magazine equivalent: place fractions on a spaceship fuel gauge number line.

**Digital:** Khan Academy's fraction number line tool; Matific (Australian); Prodigy (has good fraction modules). Evidence base: adaptive digital tools show 15–20% improvement in fraction ordering tasks (Roschelle et al., 2016).

### Story-Based Learning Hooks
The most powerful hooks for fractions involve **fairness** (younger) and **scarcity/resource management** (older):
- "There are 3 astronauts and only 1 energy bar — how do we share it fairly?" (Years 1–2)
- "The spaceship fuel tank is ¾ full — will we make it to the next planet?" (Years 4–5)
- "The alien market only trades in fractions — negotiate to get the parts you need." (Year 6)

### What Makes a Great Hook
1. **Concrete need** — the fraction matters for something to happen (not just abstract calculation)
2. **Fairness or scarcity** — emotionally resonant
3. **Visual anchor** — fraction bars on a fuel gauge, food split between characters
4. **Character with a wrong answer** — prompts readers to engage ("Is Captain Zara right?")

### Real-World Contexts That Resonate (By Age)
- **Ages 5–7:** Food sharing, sticker collections, sport scoreboards (half time)
- **Ages 8–10:** Recipes, music (beats in a bar), building/crafting, sport statistics
- **Ages 10–12:** Finance (¼ of prize money), probability, scale models, science (concentrations)

## 5. Narrative Context Library

### 🚀 Space Rationing (Ages 8–12)
The crew of a spacecraft must divide limited food, fuel, or oxygen supplies fairly.
- **Why it works:** Scarcity + fairness + high stakes = maximum engagement. Fractions become urgent.
- **Cognitive anchor:** Part-whole model naturally maps to "portion of supplies"
- **Gender note:** Make the commander, scientist, and pilot any gender; name characters from diverse backgrounds.

### 🍕 Alien Food Festival (Ages 5–8)
Aliens each eat different fractions of the feast. Who ate the most? Who got a fair share?
- **Why it works:** Food is universally familiar; alien context makes it novel and fun.
- **Gender note:** Cooking/food contexts should not default to female characters. Any character can cook.

### 🏆 Space Olympics Scoreboard (Ages 8–11)
Athletes score fractions of points across events. Order the scoreboard, find the winner.
- **Why it works:** Competition is motivating; leaderboards are naturally ordered (connects to number line).
- **Gender note:** Mix genders across all sports, including "tough" ones.

### 🎵 Rhythm Station (Ages 7–10)
A space DJ has to fill exactly 1 bar of music. They have note tiles worth ½, ¼, ⅛ of a beat.
- **Why it works:** Music is cross-cultural, tactile, and fractions map beautifully to note values.
- **Gender note:** Any character can be a musician.

### 🌱 Hydroponics Bay (Ages 8–12)
The ship grows food in a grid. What fraction of the grid is used for each vegetable? What fraction is empty?
- **Why it works:** Real science (hydroponics on ISS is real), area model is literal, allows many calculation types.
- **Gender note:** Science/botany — anyone can be the botanist.

### 🗺️ Map Reading (Ages 9–12)
An explorer must travel ¾ of the way across a planet's surface. The map shows the total distance. How far is that?
- **Why it works:** Fractions of distances on a number line; connects measurement.
- **Gender note:** Explorer/cartographer — any gender.

### 🧪 Lab Potions / Chemical Mix (Ages 10–12)
A scientist needs exactly ⅔ of a beaker of chemical X and ¼ of chemical Y to make a fuel catalyst.
- **Why it works:** Chemistry context introduces fractions with unlike denominators in a natural way.
- **Gender note:** Scientist/engineer — actively diverse.

### 🎲 Probability Spinner (Ages 10–12)
A game spinner is divided into coloured sections. What fraction of the spinner is each colour? What's the probability of landing on blue?
- **Why it works:** Connects fractions to probability; highly game-relevant.
- **Gender note:** Game/coding context — all genders represented as players and designers.
