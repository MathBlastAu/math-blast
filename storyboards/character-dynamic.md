## THE CORE DYNAMIC

Jake and Blaze are **the engine and the steering wheel.** 

Neither is complete without the other — and crucially, neither *knows* they need the other quite as much as they do. That obliviousness is part of what makes them work. They don't have a neatly defined relationship that they've discussed and agreed on. They just... fit. The way a wrench fits a bolt.

| Jake | Blaze |
|------|-------|
| Charges in | Plans the route |
| Trial and error | Systematic approach |
| "Let's try it and see" | "Let me check the variables first" |
| Loud victories | Quiet satisfaction |
| Makes things happen fast | Makes sure they happen *right* |
| Sees the big fun picture | Sees the detail that changes everything |
| Openly uncertain | Privately uncertain |
| Learns by doing | Learns by understanding |

They are not opposites who clash. They are **complementary opposites who create something neither could make alone.** The difference matters.

## HOW THEY'D SOLVE THE SAME PROBLEM DIFFERENTLY

**The Problem:** A locked gate requires a code. The code is the answer to: *"There are 6 boxes. Each box holds 8 gems. How many gems altogether?"*

### Jake's approach:
Jake reads it, makes a face, then starts counting aloud by 8s on his fingers: *"Eight, sixteen, twenty-four..."* Gets to 32, loses count, goes back to 8 and starts again more carefully. Gets to 48. Writes it down. Checks it by drawing 6 boxes and writing 8 in each one, then counting everything up. Gets 48. Types 48 into the gate immediately.

Total time: about 90 seconds. One restart. Correct.

### Blaze's approach:
Blaze reads it and identifies it immediately as a multiplication problem. She writes: `6 × 8 = ?`. Recalls 6×8 = 48. Types it in.

Total time: about 8 seconds. Correct.

### What this looks like in a scene:
**Jake:** *(counting)* "Eight, sixteen, twenty-four, thirty-two... wait. Did I say forty? Okay back to thirty-two, forty—"

**Blaze:** *(has already typed it)* "Forty-eight."

**Jake:** *(still counting on fingers)* "—and forty-eight. Yes! Wait — you already got it?"

**Blaze:** "Mm."

**Jake:** *(beat)* "Could you just... wait for me sometimes? I'm learning here."

**Blaze:** *(beat)* "48. 6 times 8. Might be worth remembering."

**Jake:** "I KNOW 6 times 8. I was just *checking*."

**Blaze:** "Sure."

**Jake:** "I was!"

*(The gate opens. They both go through it. Jake mutters "forty-eight" to himself three more times.)*

## 3 SAMPLE SCENES SHOWING THEM WORKING TOGETHER

---

### Scene 1: The Division Problem in Space
*(Issue 3, Space Arc — they've collected 36 fuel cells and need to divide them equally between 4 compartments before the ship can re-engage.)*

**Blaze:** "Thirty-six fuel cells. Four compartments. Each compartment needs an equal share."

**Jake:** "So we're dividing? 36 divided by 4?"

**Blaze:** "Yes."

**Jake:** "Okay so..." *(scrunches face)* "...what times 4 gives 36? Um. 4 times 8 is 32. 4 times 9 is... 36. Nine! Nine each!"

**Blaze:** "Correct."

**Jake:** "YES — wait, did I say that right? 4 times 9 is 36?"

**Blaze:** "Yes."

**Jake:** *(quickly putting cells into compartments)* "Nine, nine, nine, nine. Perfect."

**Blaze:** "You used the inverse to check the division."

**Jake:** "The in-what?"

**Blaze:** "You found the division answer using multiplication. That's called using the inverse."

**Jake:** "...I did? Huh." *(thoughtfully)* "Actually yeah, I did do that. Nice."

**Blaze:** "It is nice."

*(Tiny smile from Blaze. Jake doesn't see it but Bolt does.)*

### Scene 2: The Jungle Map Problem
*(Issue 3, Jungle Arc — they have a map with a scale of 1cm = 10m and need to measure the distance to the river crossing.)*

**Jake:** "Okay, the map says the river is... I've got 7 centimetres from here to the X."

**Blaze:** "And the scale is 1 centimetre equals 10 metres."

**Jake:** "So seven times ten—"

**Blaze:** "70 metres."

**Jake:** "BLAZE. I was going to say that."

**Blaze:** "You were about to use your fingers."

**Jake:** "...I find fingers helpful."

**Blaze:** "Seven times ten is always a zero added to the end of the seven. 70. It's not a finger situation."

**Jake:** *(looking at his hands)* "I mean, my fingers got me here—"

**Blaze:** "Jake. 70 metres. Can we go?"

**Jake:** "Yes. 70 metres." *(starts walking, then turns back)* "What if it was 7 times 9?"

**Blaze:** *(without turning around)* "63."

**Jake:** "What about 7 times 12?"

**Blaze:** "84. Keep walking."

**Jake:** *(walking fast to catch up)* "You're like a calculator but more fun."

**Blaze:** "Nobody has ever said that before."

**Jake:** "I'm full of surprises."

### Scene 3: The Pyramid Chamber Puzzle
*(Issue 6, Pyramids Arc — a three-part logic and maths puzzle. They need all three solutions to open the door.)*

**Blaze:** "Okay. Three puzzles, one door. Divide and conquer. I'll take the pattern sequence, you take the shape count, and we both work on the fraction one after."

**Jake:** "What's a fraction one?"

**Blaze:** "It'll be obvious when you see it." *(reads her puzzle)* "The sequence is 4, 8, 16, 32... next number."

**Jake:** *(already examining a carved wall covered in shapes)* "I've got a wall with... heaps of shapes. Circles, triangles, and squares. I need to know how many of EACH TYPE."

**Blaze:** "Counting and sorting. Systematic grid — start top left, move right, drop a row."

**Jake:** "Good call." *(starts)* "Okay counting... systematically..."

*(Pause. Both working quietly.)*

**Blaze:** "64. The sequence doubles each time: 4, 8, 16, 32, 64."

**Jake:** "Still counting—"

**Blaze:** "Take your time."

**Jake:** *(surprised)* "Did you just say take your time?"

**Blaze:** "If you rush the count you'll get it wrong and have to redo it. Take your time."

**Jake:** *(beat)* "...Thanks." *(continues, more carefully)* "Eight circles, eleven triangles, six squares."

**Blaze:** "What's the fraction puzzle?"

**Jake:** "Oh! It says... 'three-quarters of 24 gems are gold. How many gold gems?'"

**Blaze:** "Three-quarters of 24. How do we find a fraction of a number?"

**Jake:** "Divide by the bottom number, times by the top?"

**Blaze:** "Yes. Bottom number is—"

**Jake:** "4. So 24 divided by 4 is 6. Times by 3 is 18. Eighteen gold gems."

*(Blaze looks at him.)*

**Jake:** "What?"

**Blaze:** "Nothing. You're getting good at this."

**Jake:** *(quietly pleased)* "Yeah?"

**Blaze:** "Yeah." *(starts entering the answers)* "Enter your shapes."

**Jake:** "On it."

*(The door rumbles open. They look at each other. It's a good moment.)*

## THEIR BANTER STYLE

The Jake/Blaze banter is **funny but warm, never mean.** This is non-negotiable. They tease each other, but the teasing always comes from a place of genuine affection, never superiority.

**The rules:**
1. **Blaze can be deadpan about Jake's chaos — but she's never cruel.** She's pointing out reality, not attacking him.
2. **Jake can tease Blaze about being intense or robotic — but it's always affectionate.** He thinks she's incredible. The teasing comes from that.
3. **They never make each other feel stupid.** Confused, yes. Slow, no.
4. **Blaze builds Jake up when it matters.** Especially in front of other people. She's protective of him in ways she'd never admit.
5. **Jake has Blaze's back in every physical situation.** No hesitation.

**Signature Banter Beats:**

*The Deadline Beat* — Blaze gives an instruction at speed; Jake pretends to have been just about to do that same thing:
> **Blaze:** "Multiply by 6."
> **Jake:** "I was going to say multiply by 6."
> **Blaze:** "..."
> **Jake:** "I was."

*The Understatement Beat* — Jake does something impressive; Blaze gives the smallest possible acknowledgement:
> **Jake:** *(after solving a hard problem, first try)* "Get IN!"
> **Blaze:** "Mm."
> **Jake:** "That's it? I get a 'mm'?"
> **Blaze:** "It was a good mm."
> **Jake:** "It was a great mm. It was an excellent mm and you know it."

*The Reversal Beat* — Blaze does something unexpectedly Jake-like; Jake is delighted:
> **Blaze:** *(after an answer comes out wrong)* "Okay that's annoying."
> **Jake:** "DID YOU JUST SAY 'ANNOYING'?"
> **Blaze:** "Can we focus—"
> **Jake:** "You're *human*! I always suspected!"

*The Quiet Agreement Beat* — a problem stumps both of them; they sit in silence, then solve it at the same moment:
> **Jake:** "Wait — is it—"
> **Blaze:** "72."
> **Jake:** "72."
> *(Beat.)*
> **Jake:** "We're a good team."
> **Blaze:** "We're a fine team."
> **Jake:** "That's the same thing coming from you."
> **Blaze:** *(tiny smile)* "It is."

## WHY KIDS WILL WANT TO BE EITHER ONE OF THEM

### Why kids want to be Jake:
Jake is **possible**. He makes mistakes and keeps going. He finds maths hard and does it anyway. He trips over things, says the wrong thing, rushes when he should slow down — and he's still the hero of the story. Kids who find maths scary see Jake and think: *he's like me, and he figures it out. So maybe I can too.* Jake's victories are relatable victories. They feel earned because they are.

He's also just genuinely funny and loveable, and there's nothing wrong with wanting to be the funny, loveable one.

### Why kids want to be Blaze:
Blaze is **aspirational**. She makes maths look cool — not in a "we have to make maths look cool" way, but in the way that watching anyone be genuinely excellent at something is cool. She's calm. She has a plan. She's rarely scared (and when she is, she handles it). She's a girl who leads, solves, directs, and wins — without it ever being a big deal that she's a girl doing those things.

She's also dry and funny in a way that kids recognise as sophisticated, and there's nothing wrong with wanting to be the sophisticated one.

### Why they work as a pair:
Every kid reading Math Blast will identify slightly more with one or the other — and that's the point. Jake is for the kids who feel like maths isn't their thing. Blaze is for the kids who want to be better at it. Together they show that *both approaches are valid* and that maths isn't something you're born knowing — it's something you figure out, one problem at a time, better with a good partner.

## THE THING THEY NEVER SAY (BUT BOTH KNOW)

Neither Jake nor Blaze ever says "I need you" directly — because they're 10 and that's not how 10-year-olds talk. But it's visible in the text if you look for it:

- Jake always waits for Blaze before he enters an unknown space
- Blaze always checks that Jake understood before she moves on
- When one of them is in danger, the other drops everything
- They argue about methods but never about whether to help each other
- At the end of every issue, they've both done something the other couldn't have done alone

That's the partnership. It doesn't need to be stated. The reader will feel it.
