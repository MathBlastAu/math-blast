🌿 Chapter 1 — Crash into the Canopy

Blaze Lands (Hard) in Branchwick!

The vine had felt solid enough.

Ten seconds into her swing across the ravine, Blaze Chen-Okoro heard a sound she absolutely did not want to hear: SNAP.

She crashed through three layers of leaves, bounced off a thick branch, and tumbled onto something wooden and solid. A platform. She sat up, adjusted her teal jumpsuit, and looked around.

She was in a village — but not on the ground. She was way up in the canopy, on a platform built between four enormous trees. Rope bridges stretched between more platforms, disappearing into the green. Tiny creatures — bright blue, no taller than her knee, with three waving antennae — were staring at her.

"Interesting," Blaze said, brushing bark off her sleeve.

The largest of the creatures stepped forward. He wore a tiny crown made of woven vines and had the most serious expression Blaze had ever seen on something the size of a pineapple.

"I am Chief Pip," he announced. "You have fallen into Branchwick." He paused. "We have a problem."

Y3 Question 1 — Sharing Equally
Chief Pip clicked his antennae and three Sprockets rushed forward, each carrying a small basket. Together, they tipped out 12 welcome-berries onto the platform — a Branchwick custom for visitors.

"We share equally," said Chief Pip, watching Blaze carefully. "You are the third visitor today. There are 3 visitors and 12 berries. How many berries does each visitor receive?"
🍓 Chapter 2 — The Berry Harvest

Divide the Harvest — or No One Eats!

"Four each," Blaze said, before Chief Pip even finished explaining the welcome-berry custom. She raised an eyebrow. "I've already calculated that."

The Sprockets exchanged glances. Chief Pip's antennae twitched.

"Then you may be exactly the person we need," he said. "Come. There is no time."
Chief Pip led Blaze across three rope bridges and up a spiral staircase carved into the biggest tree she'd ever seen. They emerged into a wide platform — the Branchwick harvest room. Baskets were stacked everywhere, filled with berries and fruits that seemed to glow from the inside.

"Today's harvest," said Chief Pip. "It must be divided between all four families before sundown. Sprocket custom is absolute: if food is not divided fairly and exactly, no one eats until it's fixed."

He gestured at a small empty chair near the largest basket. "Our chief berry-counter — Doz — handles all the division. But this morning she went out to count the lower-canopy berries and..." Chief Pip's antennae drooped slightly. "...she has not returned."

Blaze's wrist device flickered to life, projecting a soft holographic display above her hand. Numbers. She liked numbers.

"Show me the baskets," she said.

Y3 Question 2 — Sharing by Drawing
The first basket held 24 glow-berries. These must be shared equally between 4 families.

How many glow-berries does each family receive?

💡 Tip: Try drawing 4 circles and dealing the berries one by one — 1, 2, 3, 4, 1, 2, 3, 4...
Blaze's wrist device chimed softly as she logged the result. 6 glow-berries per family. A small Sprocket named Twit carefully placed them into four leaf-lined boxes. Perfect piles.

"Next basket," said Blaze, already moving.

Y3 Question 3 — The Multiplication Link
The second basket has 18 moon-figs. They need to be shared equally between 6 families.

How many moon-figs per family? And which multiplication fact proves your answer?

💡 Tip: Division and multiplication are linked! If 18 ÷ 6 = ?, then 6 × ? = 18.
🔎 Chapter 3 — The Missing Doz

Grouping for the Medic!

Chief Pip had sent two scouts to look for Doz. They hadn't come back either — but a runner arrived breathless on the platform and said Doz was fine: she'd found a huge cluster of healing leaves far out on the eastern branches and had been packing them into medicine pouches all morning.

"She forgot to come back," Chief Pip explained. "She does this."

A second runner arrived, this one carrying a large leaf. On it, Doz had scrawled instructions in her careful handwriting: Pack the healing leaves into pouches for the medic. Groups of five. Count how many pouches. Important!

Chief Pip looked at Blaze. "This is a different kind of dividing," he said quietly. "Not sharing between families. Grouping into bundles."

"Quotitive division," Blaze said, tapping her wrist device. "I know. Let's go."

Y3 Question 4 — Grouping (Quotitive Division)
Doz has collected 20 healing leaves. The medic needs them packed into pouches with exactly 5 leaves in each pouch.

How many pouches will there be?

💡 This is different! We're not sharing between groups — we're asking: "How many groups of 5 fit into 20?"
Blaze lined the pouches up on a large leaf. Perfect. "Four pouches," she reported into her wrist device. "Logged."

Twit appeared again, this time with a smaller bundle. "More leaves," he said breathlessly. "These ones are different — groups of three, Doz says. She left another note."

Y3 Question 5 — The Full Fact Family
There are 15 leaves to be packed into groups of 3.

How many groups are there? And which complete fact family does this belong to?

💡 A fact family uses the same 3 numbers. Can you write all 4 facts: two multiplications and two divisions?
🍽️ Chapter 4 — The Feast Begins

Fire-Nuts, Bark-Bread, and a Leftover Problem!

By the time they returned to the main platform, the cooking Sprockets had been busy. The smell of roasted fire-nuts and fresh-baked bark-bread drifted across the canopy. Blaze's stomach reminded her that she hadn't eaten since breakfast.

But there was still work to do.

Nine Sprockets were seated at the long vine-table — the entire harvest crew, ready for the meal. The food needed to be divided. No one could start eating until every plate was correct. Chief Pip stood at the head of the table with his arms folded, watching Blaze with those enormous blue eyes.

"I'll go fast," Blaze told him.

"Please do," said Chief Pip. Several stomachs rumbled in agreement.

How many fire-nuts does each Sprocket get?
"Four each," Blaze announced. Twit was already scooping the fire-nuts onto plates before she finished speaking. Then came the bark-bread.

Y3 Question 7 — Bark-Bread & Pattern Spotting
Now there are 32 pieces of bark-bread to share between 8 Sprockets.

How many pieces each?

💡 Notice: Q6 was 36 ÷ 9 = ? and Q7 is 32 ÷ 8 = ? Both have the same answer! Why do you think that happens?
"Four again," said Blaze, raising an eyebrow. "Interesting."

Chief Pip allowed the faintest smile. "Division is full of patterns."

Then came the final item — a large bowl of mixed celebration berries. Chief Pip's expression became more serious.

"These are shared between all five families," he said. "Not nine Sprockets — five families. Including the Fernmoss guests who arrived this morning." He counted the berries twice. Then a third time. His antennae drooped.

"There is... a complication," he said.

🌑 Chapter 5 — Tangle's First Footprint

Something Enormous Has Been Here.

The feast had barely started when a small Sprocket named Nib came pelting across the rope bridge, antennae spinning wildly.

"Chief! The vine road to Fernmoss — vines are missing! Cut, not snapped!"

Blaze set down her plate. Her wrist device was already lit up. She followed Chief Pip to the eastern edge of the platform. The vine bridge that should have stretched to the next tree was gone — not worn away or broken. The ends were clean. Cut deliberately.

And on the wide branch below, in the thick moss, was a footprint.

It was enormous. Larger than Blaze's whole body. Three-toed, with deep pressure marks. Whatever made it was heavy.

"What is that?" Blaze asked quietly.

Chief Pip's voice was very small. "We don't know. We call it Tangle." He paused. "The Fernmoss family who came to visit today... we cannot reach them now. And the vine road back is gone."

Blaze pressed her lips together. She looked at the footprint. She looked at her wrist device. Then she looked at Chief Pip.

"Give me five seconds," she said, and started calculating.

Y3 Question 9 — Vine Bundles
The Sprockets have stored 28 spare vines in the repair shed. To rebuild a bridge they need to bundle them into groups of 7.

How many bundles can they make?
"Four bundles," Blaze said. "That's enough to make a temporary bridge. But there's another problem."

She pulled up a map on her wrist device — a rough grid of all the food portions they'd prepared, and a new question from Chief Pip.

"We prepared extra food for the Fernmoss family before we knew the bridge was out," he said. "It needs to be redistributed to all six Branchwick families. Everything. And the medic gets whatever is left over, for the injured scouts."

"That's the boss round, isn't it?" Blaze said.

Chief Pip's antennae wiggled. "Is it?"

"It feels like one," she said. "Let's go."

✨ Key Concept: Two Kinds of Division

Partitive: "Share 24 between 4 groups — how many each?" (Q1, Q2, Q6, Q7)

Quotitive: "How many groups of 5 fit into 20?" (Q4, Q5, Q9)

Both are division — but the question feels different! Most kids learn partitive first.

🔁 Remainders — What's the Big Idea?

Q8 introduces the concept that division doesn't always work perfectly. 24 ÷ 5 = 4 remainder 4.

In Q10, the meaning of the remainder matters — it goes to the medic! This is real-world reasoning, not just arithmetic.

💬 Conversation Starters

  • After Q3 (multiplication link): "If 6 × 3 = 18, what does that tell us about 18 ÷ 6? And 18 ÷ 3? Try to find all four facts in the family."
  • After Q7 (pattern spotting): "36 ÷ 9 = 4 and 32 ÷ 8 = 4. Can you find another division that gives 4? How about 28 ÷ 7? What's the pattern?"
  • After Q8 (first remainder): "What would happen if there were 25 berries and 5 families? What about 26? 27? 28? 29? 30? What do you notice about the remainders?"
  • After Q10 (boss): "The medic got 3 extras. When is a remainder a problem, and when is it actually useful?"

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Watch For

  • Mixing up the two kinds of division (Q4): Some kids get confused when switching from "sharing" to "grouping". Use physical objects — 20 counters, make groups of 5 and count how many groups you get.
  • Fact families (Q5): Kids often write 3 facts instead of 4. Remind them: two multiplications (3×5 and 5×3) and two divisions (15÷3 and 15÷5).
  • Remainders (Q8): A common error is writing the remainder as the answer. Stress: the answer is how many EACH, then separately: how many are left.
  • Boss round (Q10): 45 ÷ 6 — kids may try 8 (8×6=48, too high) or 6 (too low). Encourage the "try and check" method: 7×6=42, that leaves 3 left over.

📚 How This Aligns with School

This issue covers ACARA Australian Curriculum v9.0 — Number strand, primarily Year 3. The key achievement standard addressed: "Students can solve problems involving multiplication and division, using arrays, number lines, and grouping strategies."

The introduction of remainders is a Year 3–4 bridge concept. If your child found Q8 and Q10 challenging, that's expected and normal — practice with physical objects (coins, snacks) before returning.
