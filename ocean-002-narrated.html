🌊 Chapter 1 — The Outer Fields

Beyond the City, the Lattice Stretched for Miles.

Press ▶ to hear the story
The next morning, Elder Luma sent a guide. Wren was young for a Coralfolk keeper, nervous and eager, the amber patterns on her shell still bright and unfaded. She led Marina and Flick out through the edge of Luminos and into the vast open fields of the outer Lattice.

Out here, the ocean floor was almost flat. Row after row of glowing signal stones stretched as far as Marina could see, arranged in a grid that disappeared into the dark water ahead. Flick darted between the rows, antenna fins pointing in every direction at once.

"The Lattice is largest out here," Wren said, in a small, precise voice. "We count the stones by skipping. It is the fastest way, and the way that matches how the signal travels. You will see."

💡 Chapter 2 — The Pattern in the Stones

Every Skip Was a Multiplication in Disguise.

Press ▶ to hear the story
Wren moved along the first row of stones, touching every second one. Marina matched her step for step. "When you count by twos," Wren explained, "you are not just skipping. You are counting groups of two. Five skips is five groups of two. Five times two."

Marina checked her datapad. "So skip counting is multiplication. Counting by fives is the same as multiplying by five. Counting by tens is multiplying by ten."

"Exactly," said Wren, with a small click of her shell that Marina was starting to recognise as approval. "The Lattice uses this to send signals fast. When a stone sends to two stones, and those two each send to two more, the count doubles every step. Skip by skip, group by group."

🔢 Chapter 3 — Groups of Ten

The Fastest Way to Count a Grid.

Press ▶ to hear the story
The outer field opened up into the largest section of all. The stones here stretched on and on, arranged in long rows of ten. Marina craned her neck to see the far end, but the glow simply faded into dark water in the distance.

"This is the main transmission grid," said Wren, with quiet pride. "Thousands of stones, in groups of ten. We count by tens out here because a single signal can jump ten stones at once."

Marina knelt down to study her datapad. Groups of ten were easy to count. Ten, twenty, thirty. The signal would be easy to track. But something in the pattern looked wrong. Three sections were glowing steadily, but the fourth was dark. Something was interrupting the flow.

📊 Chapter 4 — Tracing the Fault

Marina's Datapad Told the Story in Numbers.

Press ▶ to hear the story
Marina's wrist datapad mapped the fault line. The disruption started at a specific cluster of stones, and the skip-count pattern broke there. Instead of jumping by tens, the signal was jumping by irregular amounts. Something down below was throwing it off.

Flick pressed close to Wren, wing-tips flaring with concern. Wren reached out a small three-fingered hand and patted Flick's underbelly. It helped a little.

"We can work out how many stones should be active in each section," Marina said. "If I know the number of groups and how many in each group, I can calculate the total and compare it to what's actually glowing. That will show us exactly where the fault is."

⚡ Chapter 5 — The Shadow

Something Had Blocked Out the Light Above Them.

Press ▶ to hear the story
Marina was mid-calculation when the light changed. The filtered shafts of blue that reached down from the surface, far, far above, dimmed suddenly. A shadow moved across the ocean floor. Enormous. Silent. Moving slowly and deliberately from east to west.

Flick went completely still. Even Flick's antenna fins were flat against its body. Wren pressed herself against a nearby stone and did not move.

The shadow passed. The light returned. Marina released a breath she hadn't known she was holding. Then the shadow came back.

Marina and Wren stood frozen, both of them looking upward. The shadow had come back. Larger this time, or perhaps closer. It moved with slow, deliberate patience, blotting out the filtered light until the ocean floor around them was near-black.

"Wren," Marina said, very quietly, "what kind of creature makes a shadow that big?"

Wren was silent for a long moment. When she finally spoke, her voice was barely above a whisper.

"In all my lifetimes of working the Lattice, and all the records of all the keepers before me, nothing that size has ever been seen. Not once. Not in living memory."

The shadow passed. Below the ocean floor, the deep, rhythmic hum continued, patient as a heartbeat.
