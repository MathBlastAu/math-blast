# CHARACTER BIBLE — JAKE NOVA
## *Math Blast* Space-Adventure Math Magazine
### Years 3–4 | Ages 8–10 | Australian Curriculum

---

> **Document Purpose:** This character bible defines Jake Nova as a consistent, living character across all Math Blast issues and adventure worlds. Writers and artists should treat this as the authoritative guide to how Jake thinks, speaks, moves, and grows.

---

## 1. CORE IDENTITY

**Full Name:** Jake Nova
*(Nova — because he burns bright and sometimes makes a big mess doing it)*

**Age:** 10

**Birthday:** 4 July — the same day as a major rocket launch. Jake was born in the waiting room of the Canberra Space Command Centre while his mum was there for a science conference. He considers this "basically being born in space."

**Hometown:** Canberra, ACT, Australia — specifically a suburb just close enough to the Canberra Deep Space Communication Complex that he could see the giant satellite dishes from his bedroom window.

### Backstory
Jake grew up obsessed with space. Not the glamorous kind — the real, chaotic, brilliant kind. His dad is a mechanic and his mum is a maths teacher (which Jake considers deeply unfair), and between the two of them he grew up pulling things apart and putting them back together, usually with a few extra screws left over.

He applied for the Junior Space Cadet Programme three times. Failed the maths test twice. The third time, he passed — barely — and was so excited he knocked the interviewer's coffee off the desk with his elbow. They gave him the spot anyway. They said they'd never seen a kid want it more.

He met Blaze at orientation. She had already memorised the mission manual. He had brought the wrong bag.

**Core Personality (3 words):** Brave, Chaotic, Big-hearted

**Greatest Strength:** Jake never gives up. When things go wrong — and they always go wrong for Jake — he doesn't freeze or walk away. He digs in, tries again, tries something completely different, tries standing on his head if he has to. His persistence is almost comically unstoppable.

**Greatest Flaw / Weakness:** He acts before he thinks. Jake's first instinct is always *do something* — which sometimes solves the problem brilliantly and sometimes causes a second, worse problem. He also finds it hard to ask for help because he doesn't want to look dumb, which is ironic because asking for help is exactly what makes him not dumb.

**What He Wants Most (Surface Goal):** To be a proper space explorer — brave, skilled, and the kind of kid people write books about.

**What He Needs Most (Deeper Emotional Need):** To realise that making mistakes doesn't mean he's not good enough. The things that make him feel embarrassed — getting the maths wrong, tripping over, not knowing the answer — are the same things that make him real, relatable, and ultimately brilliant.

**Favourite Saying / Catchphrase:**
> *"Alright, alright — I've got this."*
*(Said mostly to convince himself. Sometimes it works.)*

Secondary catchphrase (when things have gone spectacularly wrong):
> *"...So that's not what was supposed to happen."*

## 2. VOICE & TONE

### How Jake Speaks
Jake talks the way most 9–10 year olds actually talk — fast, enthusiastic, slightly scattered. He doesn't always finish his sentences when he's excited. He uses Australian slang naturally: *heaps, reckon, yeah nah, deadly* (as a compliment), *mad* (as a compliment). He calls maths problems "the puzzle" when he's feeling confident and "the nightmare" when he's not.

- Sentence rhythm: punchy, energetic, sometimes runs on
- Vocabulary: solid for his age, but stretches to use new words he's just learned — and sometimes gets them slightly wrong in a charming way
- Quirks: talks to himself when he's working through a problem; makes sound effects (*"three, two, one — boom!"*); narrates what he's doing when nervous

### When Things Go Wrong
> *"Oh no no no no no—"*
> *"...Right. Okay. That's fine. That's totally fine."*
> *"How did that even — you know what, doesn't matter. New plan."*

### When He Gets Something Right
> *"YES! I knew it — I KNEW IT!"*
> *"Oh that felt good. Did that feel good to you? That felt good to me."*
> *"GET IN! Okay. What's next?"*

### How Jake Approaches a Math Problem
Jake's process is very human and very visible:
1. **Stares at it** — sometimes makes a pained face
2. **Tries the first thing that comes to mind** — even if it's wrong
3. **Gets something wrong, frowns, crosses it out**
4. **Makes an observation** ("Wait, that number is the same as...")
5. **Has the "oh!" moment** — usually arrives as a surprise
6. **Checks it twice because he doesn't trust his first answer** *(which is actually great mathematical habit)*

He draws arrows, circles, little rockets next to numbers. His working-out is messy but real.

### Sample Dialogue

*Jake is staring at a multiplication problem in the Jungle World. The vines are closing in. They need the answer to escape.*

**Jake:** "Okay, 7 times 8... 7 times 8... is it 54? No — wait — 54 is 6 times 9. I always mix those up."

**Blaze:** "Jake. Vines. Moving fast."

**Jake:** "I KNOW about the vines, Blaze! Okay — 7 times 8 — I'll count by sevens. Seven, fourteen, twenty-one, twenty-eight, thirty-five, forty-two, forty-nine, fifty-SIX!"

**Blaze:** "56. Yes. Now—"

**Jake:** *(already punching it in)* "FIFTY-SIX!" *(vines retract)* "I got it! Did you see that? I counted by sevens and everything!"

**Blaze:** *(smiling despite herself)* "You're impossible."

**Jake:** "Impossibly good at maths."

### Voice Arc: Years 3 → Year 4

**Early issues (Y3 tone):** Jake's dialogue is more reactive and exclamatory. He expresses doubt more openly. His victories feel big and surprising — to him as much as to the reader.

**Later issues (Y4 tone):** Jake starts to trust his instincts more. He still checks his work, but he's less surprised when he gets it right. He starts to offer his method to others — "I count by sevens, want me to show you?" — which shows his growth from uncertain to quietly confident.

## 3. RELATIONSHIPS

### Jake & Blaze
Jake and Blaze are the classic odd couple who work perfectly together — neither would be as good without the other. Jake is the engine; Blaze is the steering wheel. Jake charges in; Blaze redirects. Blaze has the plan; Jake has the nerve to execute it (sometimes too enthusiastically).

Jake genuinely admires Blaze's brain. He would never admit he's intimidated by her — but he does have a habit of repeating her instructions back to her slightly wrong, which she corrects, which means he actually understands it better. He'd never admit that either.

They bicker like siblings. But when something really goes wrong — when the stakes are real — they absolutely have each other's backs.

### Jake & Bolt (the robot sidekick from Issue #2)
Jake *loves* Bolt immediately and completely, the way only a 10-year-old can love a robot. He talks to Bolt like they're best friends, gives Bolt nicknames ("Bolty," "Big Guy," "the legendary Bolt"), and gets genuinely upset if Bolt is in danger.

Bolt sometimes gives Jake data that Jake has to interpret mathematically — which Jake pretends to find annoying but actually finds really satisfying.

Jake's first words to Bolt: *"Can you fly? Please say you can fly."*

### Jake & Mission Control / Adults
Jake respects adults but is slightly allergic to being told to slow down. He listens to Mission Control but also... interprets their instructions liberally. Commander Chen (Mission Control lead) has Jake's file flagged with a sticky note that reads: *"Enthusiastic. Monitor closely."*

Jake's relationship with adults softens across the series — he starts to understand that taking advice isn't weakness. By the Pyramids world, he's actually asking questions before acting, which Commander Chen privately considers a miracle.

### Jake with Kids Struggling at Maths
This is where Jake shines most naturally. He *is* the kid who struggled. He doesn't perform encouragement — he means it.

> *"I got this one wrong twice before I figured it out. Twice! So that's normal. Honestly the second try is usually where the good thinking happens."*

He never makes a struggling reader feel slow. He shares his own wrong answers freely, without embarrassment, because he genuinely doesn't see being wrong as shameful — he sees it as the thing that happens before being right.

## 4. CHARACTER ARC ACROSS THE 4 ADVENTURE WORLDS

---

### WORLD 1: SPACE 🚀
*(Issues 1–7 of the Space arc)*

**What this world brings out in Jake:**
Space is Jake's *dream* — which means it's also where his insecurities are loudest. He wants so badly to be good here. The gap between who he imagines himself to be and who he actually is (a somewhat clumsy, slightly panicky kid who really struggles with long multiplication) is at its most visible.

**Emotional Journey:**
- *Issue 1:* Jake crash-lands. He's scared but pushes through. The first maths problem he solves feels like a massive win because he had no idea if he could.
- *Issues 2–3:* Gains confidence. Starts trusting his instincts. Makes a medium-sized mistake in Issue 3 that has real consequences (they take a wrong route through an asteroid field because he misread coordinates). He has to own it.
- *Issues 4–5:* Learns from the asteroid mistake. Slows down enough to double-check. Still fast, but not reckless.
- *Issues 6–7:* Becomes the kid he wanted to be — not perfect, but capable, and aware of it. The final Space mission requires him to calculate a re-entry trajectory. He's nervous. He does it. He's right.

**What He Learns by Issue 7:**
Being a space explorer isn't about being flawless — it's about being the kind of person who can figure it out when it matters.

**Defining Moment:**
Issue 3 — Jake has to admit to Blaze that he made the navigation error. He expects her to be furious. She isn't. She says: *"I know. I saw you do it. I was going to say something but I thought you'd figure it out."* He did. That moment — being trusted to fix his own mistake — changes him.

---

### WORLD 2: JUNGLE 🌿
*(Issues 1–7 of the Jungle arc)*

**What this world brings out in Jake:**
The jungle is sensory overload — loud, wet, full of creatures and surprises. Jake *loves* it. He's in his element: something new around every corner, no time to overthink, pure instinct. But the jungle also teaches him that instinct without observation is just guessing.

**Emotional Journey:**
- *Issues 1–2:* Jake is delighted — bugs! vines! giant frogs! He treats it like an adventure playground and rushes everywhere.
- *Issues 3–4:* The jungle starts to push back. He gets lost. He realises the jungle has patterns — and that maths is how you read them.
- *Issues 5–6:* Jake starts *observing* before acting. He's not suddenly careful, but he's... curiously careful.
- *Issue 7:* He uses measurement, estimation, and pattern recognition to navigate out of the deep jungle. He finds it genuinely satisfying in a way he didn't expect.

**What He Learns by Issue 7:**
Slowing down to notice doesn't mean stopping. It means noticing more.

**Defining Moment:**
Issue 4 — Jake is separated from Blaze and has to find a path through the jungle alone using only a compass, a map scale, and his maths. He gets it right. Nobody sees him do it. He knows, and that's enough.

---

### WORLD 3: TREASURE HUNT 🗺️
*(Issues 1–7 of the Treasure Hunt arc)*

**What this world brings out in Jake:**
Jake is *made* for treasure hunts. The competitive thrill, the riddles, the sense that there's a right answer and he just has to find it — this world turns Jake's trial-and-error approach into an actual superpower.

**Emotional Journey:**
- *Issues 1–2:* Jake tears through puzzles at speed, sometimes missing clues in his excitement.
- *Issues 3–4:* Hits a hard problem — a multi-step puzzle that he can't brute-force. He has to think systematically. It's uncomfortable.
- *Issues 5–6:* Develops a hybrid approach — Blaze's systematic thinking + Jake's intuitive leaps. Together they become unstoppable.
- *Issue 7:* The final treasure requires a combination lock of three maths problems. Jake solves two of the three. He doesn't solve the third — Blaze does. He hands off perfectly, without ego. That's his real treasure.

**What He Learns by Issue 7:**
The best treasure hunters work in teams. Knowing what you're good at — and what someone else is better at — isn't weakness. It's strategy.

**Defining Moment:**
Issue 6 — Jake figures out a clue that Blaze missed because he recognised a pattern from a completely different context (a number sequence from the Space arc). He is so excited he does an actual victory lap of the room.

---

### WORLD 4: PYRAMIDS 🏺
*(Issues 1–7 of the Pyramids arc)*

**What this world brings out in Jake:**
The pyramids make Jake feel small in the best possible way. They're ancient, massive, and built on maths that has survived thousands of years. For the first time, Jake is genuinely awed — not by technology or adventure, but by *mathematics itself*.

**Emotional Journey:**
- *Issues 1–2:* Jake is wide-eyed, respectful, slightly overwhelmed by the scale of everything.
- *Issues 3–4:* Starts connecting pyramid maths to things he already knows — fractions in the layout, symmetry in the chambers, patterns in the stones. Maths starts to feel ancient and alive.
- *Issues 5–6:* Becomes something of a guide — explaining things to Blaze (who is more data-focused) in human terms. Their roles gently reverse.
- *Issue 7:* Jake solves the final chamber puzzle using intuition *informed* by everything he's learned. He's not just guessing — he's reasoning. He doesn't know when that changed.

**What He Learns by Issue 7:**
Maths isn't just something you do in school. It's in everything. It always was. He just needed to look.

**Defining Moment:**
Issue 5 — Jake runs his hand along a carved wall and says, quietly, *"Someone did the maths for this thousands of years ago. Like, an actual person sat down and figured it out."* Blaze looks at him. It's the most thoughtful thing he's ever said. He's a bit embarrassed. She doesn't make fun of him.

## 5. VISUAL DESIGN NOTES

### Signature Colours
**Orange** is Jake's colour — warm, energetic, slightly chaotic. It's the colour of rocket fire and autumn leaves and the inside of a bright idea. It matches his personality perfectly: impossible to ignore, full of energy, slightly overwhelming, but you can't help smiling.

His **silver trim** grounds the orange — it's the cool-headed moments that keep him from going completely off the rails. The **blue visor** is his "thinking mode" — when the visor is down, Jake is focused.

### Consistent Visual Details (MUST REMAIN ACROSS ALL IMAGES)
- Messy mid-brown hair with a strong cowlick; never flat or neat
- Bright emerald-green eyes — expressive, wide, the most distinctive part of his face
- Freckles across nose and cheeks — not too many, just the right amount
- Orange space suit (main garment, can adapt by world — see below)
- Red rocket patch on upper left arm — present in every look
- Slightly scuffed, worn boots — Jake is not precious about his kit
- His right elbow is always slightly dinged from... various incidents

### Expression Range

| Expression | Description |
|---|---|
| **Happy** | Eyes crinkle up, big open grin, often mid-gesture (fist pump, pointing) |
| **Confused** | One eyebrow up, one down; mouth slightly open; head tilted; staring at something like it owes him an apology |
| **Determined** | Jaw set, eyes narrowed, slight lean forward — he gets very still before he goes very fast |
| **Triumphant** | Both arms up, full grin, slightly dishevelled from whatever just happened |
| **Scared** | Eyes very wide, shoulders up near ears, lips pressed together — trying to look braver than he feels |

### Across Adventure Worlds

| World | Jake's Look |
|---|---|
| **Space** | Full orange space suit with silver trim and blue helmet visor. Rocket patch prominent. Sometimes visor up, sometimes down for drama. |
| **Jungle** | Orange explorer shirt (same signature colour) over khaki shorts. Red rocket patch on shirt. Bandana around wrist. Hair extra messy from humidity. |
| **Treasure Hunt** | Orange vest over white shirt, cargo pants. Red rocket patch on vest. Worn leather satchel that he stuffs things into haphazardly. |
| **Pyramids** | Lightweight orange tunic/explorer shirt. Red rocket patch. Sand-coloured pants. Sunburned on the shoulders (he always forgets sunscreen). |

**The orange and red rocket patch must always be present.** These are Jake's visual signature.

## 6. MATH PERSONALITY

### How Jake Models Good Mathematical Thinking
Jake is the character who makes maths feel survivable. He:
- **Shows his working** — always, because he has to or he loses track
- **Counts aloud** when he's uncertain — this is modelled as *smart*, not babyish
- **Draws pictures and diagrams** — turns abstract problems into concrete images
- **Uses known facts to work out unknown ones** — he might not know 7×8 but he knows 7×7=49, so he adds one more 7
- **Checks his answer** — not because he's told to, but because he's been burned before

### Mistakes Jake Makes (and How He Recovers)
Jake makes mistakes that real kids make:
- Misreads a number (writes 63, means 36)
- Rushes and skips a step
- Confuses similar facts (6×9 vs 7×8)
- Adds when he should multiply

His recovery is always the same: he goes back, finds where he went wrong, and corrects it without drama. He says: *"Found it — right here. I wrote 36 but it should be 63. Easy fix."* He treats errors as information, not failure.

### Strategies Jake Uses
- **Counting on / counting up** (still visible in Y3, mostly replaced by Y4)
- **Skip counting** — by 2s, 5s, 10s, then pushed to 7s and 8s
- **Making tens** — natural for him, slightly intuitive
- **Drawing it out** — strong visual learner, diagrams everywhere
- **Trial and error** — tries a number, adjusts, tries again
- **Estimation first** — gets a rough answer, then refines

### Celebrating Success
Jake celebrates *loudly* and physically. Fist pumps. He says *"YES!"* He does not do subtle. On particularly good answers, he does a small victory lap if space permits. He has been asked not to do this inside the rocket.

### Handling Getting It Wrong
Jake's first reaction is a groan and a muttered *"oh come ON."* Then, crucially, he gets curious. "Where did I go wrong?" is always his second question. He has been wrong enough times to know that the wrong answer is just one step away from the right one — you just have to find where you turned off.

He never says "I can't do this." He says "I haven't got it yet."

## 7. JAKE & BLAZE TOGETHER

*→ See also: `character-dynamic.md` for the full shared section.*

### Their Dynamic in Brief
Jake and Blaze are the engine and the steering wheel. Jake generates momentum — ideas, energy, willingness to try things — and Blaze provides direction, precision, and the plans that actually work. They are not the same person and they don't need to be. They are better together than either is alone.

Jake's greatest moments happen because Blaze trusted him with something hard. Blaze's best moments happen when Jake does something unexpected that her planning didn't account for — and it works.

### Why Kids Will Want to BE Jake
Jake is the character kids who are scared of maths will recognise immediately. He gets things wrong. He groan-reads problems. He tries the wrong thing first. And then — *and this is the important part* — he figures it out anyway. He makes figuring it out look possible. Even fun.

Kids who feel like they're not "maths people" watch Jake and think: *he's like me, and he does it.* So maybe I can too.
