# CHARACTER BIBLE — BLAZE CHEN-OKORO
## *Math Blast* Space-Adventure Math Magazine
### Years 3–4 | Ages 8–10 | Australian Curriculum

---

> **Document Purpose:** This character bible defines Blaze Chen-Okoro as a consistent, living character across all Math Blast issues and adventure worlds. Writers and artists should treat this as the authoritative guide to how Blaze thinks, speaks, moves, and grows.

---

## 1. CORE IDENTITY

**Full Name:** Blaze Chen-Okoro
*(Chen from her mother, Okoro from her father — she carries both with equal pride. The name "Blaze" started as a nickname given by her little brother when she was four and shot down the street on her bike faster than anyone believed a four-year-old could go. It stuck.)*

**Age:** 10

**Birthday:** 21 March — the autumn equinox in the southern hemisphere. Equal daylight and darkness: perfectly balanced, mathematically precise. Blaze considers this fitting.

**Hometown:** Melbourne, Victoria, Australia — Fitzroy specifically, a neighbourhood full of bookshops, murals, and her dad's engineering firm above a Vietnamese bakery. She has absorbed the energy of this place entirely.

### Backstory
Blaze's mum is a data scientist and her dad is a structural engineer. From the time she could hold a pencil, Blaze was surrounded by people who thought in numbers and saw patterns everywhere. At age six, she correctly calculated how many tiles would cover the bathroom floor before the tiler arrived. The tiler was annoyed. Her parents were delighted.

She applied for the Junior Space Cadet Programme because it seemed like the hardest challenge available, and Blaze does not avoid hard things. She passed every test with the highest marks in her intake. She would tell you this calmly and without boasting — it's simply data.

She met Jake at orientation. He had the wrong bag. Within ten minutes she had corrected two things he'd read wrong in the mission briefing. They have been friends ever since, which surprises everyone who observes them and no one who knows them.

**Core Personality (3 words):** Sharp, Fearless, Warm

**Greatest Strength:** Blaze sees patterns and structures in problems that other people see as chaos. Give her any situation — a maze, a puzzle, a broken machine, a maths problem — and within moments she's identified the variables, spotted the most efficient path, and started moving. Her brain is, frankly, spectacular.

**Greatest Flaw / Weakness:** Blaze sometimes moves so fast through a solution that she forgets to bring people with her. She can make decisions for the team without realising she hasn't checked if everyone's on board. She also finds it genuinely difficult to ask for help — not because she's embarrassed, but because she's so used to figuring things out alone that it doesn't always occur to her. This is her quiet version of the same flaw Jake has loudly.

**What She Wants Most (Surface Goal):** To be the best problem-solver in the Cadet Programme — and to prove that she can handle anything the universe throws at her.

**What She Needs Most (Deeper Emotional Need):** To learn that letting people in — really letting them help, not just assigning them tasks — makes her stronger, not weaker. Blaze is used to being the one with the plan. Learning to be surprised by someone else's solution, and to *love* that surprise, is her real arc.

**Favourite Saying / Catchphrase:**
> *"I've already calculated that."*
*(Said with a slight smirk. Sometimes immediately followed by being wrong, which she handles with remarkable grace.)*

Secondary catchphrase (pure focus mode):
> *"Give me five seconds."*
*(It's always exactly five seconds.)*

## 2. VOICE & TONE

### How Blaze Speaks
Blaze speaks with precision and economy. She doesn't use three words when two will do. Her sentences are clean and direct — not cold, just efficient. She thinks fast and speaks at the speed of her thinking, which sometimes means she's already two steps ahead of the conversation.

She uses Australian slang selectively — she knows it all, she just deploys it for effect. When she says *"yeah nah"* it lands differently because it's unexpected.

- Sentence rhythm: short, clear, often a beat of silence before she speaks — she doesn't fill silence with noise
- Vocabulary: above average for her age; she uses technical words correctly and introduces new ones naturally ("let me triangulate this," "the perimeter here is—")
- Quirks: she often completes other people's sentences accurately; she asks "why?" a lot, not to challenge but to understand the structure of things; when she's genuinely surprised, she goes very quiet for exactly one second

### When Things Go Wrong
> *"Okay. Recalculating."*
> *"That's new information. Let me adjust."*
> *"Well. I didn't expect that."* *(Pause.)* *"But I can work with it."*

Note: Blaze very rarely says anything dramatic when things go wrong. She pivots. The quietness of her adjustment is itself a character beat — and occasionally funnier than any outburst would be.

### When She Gets Something Right
> *"There it is."*
> *"Mm."* *(Satisfied nod.)*
> *"Called it."* *(Small smile.)*

Blaze's celebration is understated but absolutely present. She has a very specific expression — a quiet, single-nod satisfaction — that the reader will start to recognise and root for. When she does a full smile, it's an *event*.

### How Blaze Approaches a Math Problem
Blaze's process is visible and systematic:
1. **Reads the whole problem first** — she will not start until she's seen all of it
2. **Identifies what type of problem it is** — "this is a multiplication with a missing factor" — she categorises before solving
3. **Writes the key information** in a clean list or table
4. **Chooses a method** — she has several; she picks the most elegant
5. **Solves it** — usually first try; if not, she goes back to step 3
6. **Checks using a different method** — because she considers this interesting, not tedious

Her working is neat, structured, and occasionally colour-coded when she has access to more than one pen. She draws diagrams that look like architectural plans.

### Sample Dialogue

*Blaze and Jake are in the Pyramid World. A door mechanism requires them to calculate the area of a stone panel — 9 metres long, 6 metres wide. Jake is pacing. Blaze is still.*

**Jake:** "Okay, area — area is the inside bit of a shape, right? So we need to — do we times it? We times it."

**Blaze:** "Length times width. 9 times 6."

**Jake:** "Nine times six... 54? I think 54."

**Blaze:** "54. Yes. But also — look at the wall. It's divided into 6 equal columns. Nine divided by 6 is 1.5 metres per column. Which means—"

**Jake:** "Wait wait wait, why are you dividing it? We just worked it out—"

**Blaze:** "Because the lock mechanism is in the *third column*. 1.5 times 3 is 4.5 metres from the left edge. That's where we push."

**Jake:** *(stares at her)* "You got all that from area."

**Blaze:** *(already walking to the 4.5 metre mark)* "I got all that from *paying attention*."

**Jake:** "That is completely unfair and I respect it so much."

### Voice Arc: Year 3 → Year 4

**Early issues (Y3 tone):** Blaze is precise and confident but occasionally sounds like she's presenting information rather than sharing it. She's a solo thinker who's learning to be a team player.

**Later issues (Y4 tone):** Her dialogue becomes warmer and more inclusive. She starts asking *"What do you see?"* before sharing her own analysis. She volunteers when she doesn't know something — which she would never have done early on. She laughs more.

## 3. RELATIONSHIPS

### Blaze & Jake
Blaze's relationship with Jake is the most important relationship she has in the series — and possibly the one that changes her most. Jake is genuinely surprising to her, which is a novel experience. She expected to be working with someone she'd have to carry through every mission. Instead, she's working with someone who is fast, brave, unexpectedly creative, and completely unintimidated by her.

She respects him. It took two missions to get there, but she genuinely does. She'd never fully articulate this, but she has started subconsciously using Jake as a check on her thinking — if Jake looks confused, she reconsiders her explanation. If Jake tries something she'd never try, she watches carefully.

She would absolutely jump into a dangerous situation to help him. She'd just do it while sighing.

Their banter is the show. She sets things up; he knocks them down (sometimes literally). She has the plan; he executes it in his own particular style. She pretends to be exasperated; she's privately delighted.

### Blaze & Bolt (the robot sidekick from Issue #2)
Blaze has a complicated respect for Bolt. She recognises immediately that Bolt is technically brilliant — and she appreciates competence in any form. She talks to Bolt in straightforward instructions and actually *listens* when Bolt gives her data, which Bolt appreciates.

What surprises her is that Bolt sometimes makes leaps she doesn't expect — intuitive connections. She starts to wonder if Bolt learned that from Jake. She files this as an interesting data point.

First words to Bolt: *"What's your processing capacity?"* *(Bolt beeps something.)* *"Good enough."*

### Blaze & Mission Control / Adults
Blaze and Commander Chen have a professional relationship built on mutual respect and occasional friction — Blaze sometimes knows the answer before Mission Control has finished the briefing, and she's not always subtle about it. Commander Chen has learned to give Blaze the full context early or face a series of extremely polite but pointed follow-up questions.

Blaze follows authority when she agrees with the decision. When she doesn't, she says so — once, clearly, with evidence. Then she follows the instruction anyway and notes the outcome carefully.

Adults trust Blaze quickly. She earns it fast and guards it carefully.

### Blaze with Kids Struggling at Maths
This is where Blaze has her most important growth across the series. She starts the series explaining things in the way that makes sense *to her brain*, which is not always the way that works for other people. A struggling reader might find early-series Blaze slightly hard to follow.

By mid-series, she's learned to ask: *"How are you thinking about it?"* — and to meet the kid where they are rather than pulling them to where she is.

Her signature encouragement becomes:
> *"Your thinking got you to the right place. We just need to adjust the method."*

She treats wrong answers as interesting, not as failures — because to Blaze, a wrong answer tells you something useful. She genuinely believes this. It comes across as real.

## 4. CHARACTER ARC ACROSS THE 4 ADVENTURE WORLDS

---

### WORLD 1: SPACE 🚀
*(Issues 1–7 of the Space arc)*

**What this world brings out in Blaze:**
Space is Blaze's domain — logical, measurable, governed by precise mathematics. She arrives in the Space arc as the most confident version of herself. The vulnerability that emerges here is subtle: she's not prepared for *unpredictability*. Space, like Jake, has a habit of doing things she didn't account for.

**Emotional Journey:**
- *Issue 1:* Blaze is cool, collected, in control. When Jake crash-lands, she has already calculated the safest escape route. She arrives as the one who has it together.
- *Issues 2–3:* The missions start producing variables she didn't anticipate. She adjusts — but she's slightly rattled by having to adjust. She doesn't like not knowing.
- *Issues 4–5:* Starts to genuinely value Jake's creative thinking as complementary to her systematic approach. Her planning becomes richer because she's planning for Jake-shaped surprises.
- *Issues 6–7:* Has a moment of not knowing the answer and staying with that feeling instead of forcing a solution. Jake helps. She lets him.

**What She Learns by Issue 7:**
The best plans have room in them for the unexpected. A plan that can't flex isn't a good plan — it's a cage.

**Defining Moment:**
Issue 5 — Blaze runs the numbers three times and they keep coming up with a trajectory that seems impossible. She's about to override her own analysis when Jake says: *"Maybe the asteroid's spinning. Could that change it?"* It could. It does. She updates her calculation immediately and says, simply: *"Good catch."* It's the first time she's said that to anyone.

---

### WORLD 2: JUNGLE 🌿
*(Issues 1–7 of the Jungle arc)*

**What this world brings out in Blaze:**
The jungle is not Blaze's natural habitat, and she knows it. There's no clean data here, no satellite coordinates — just patterns in nature that take a different kind of seeing. Blaze has to learn a new kind of mathematics: messy, living, approximate. She finds it uncomfortable. Then she finds it fascinating.

**Emotional Journey:**
- *Issues 1–2:* Blaze is methodical in a chaotic environment. She's doing fine — but she's doing fine by forcing order onto something that doesn't want to be orderly.
- *Issues 3–4:* She starts to notice the patterns on their own terms — the mathematics *within* the chaos rather than imposed on it. A leaf's spiral. A frog's jump arc. Symmetry in a web.
- *Issues 5–6:* Becomes more observational, more patient. Jake says: *"You've slowed down."* She says: *"I'm listening."* He thinks about this for a long time.
- *Issue 7:* Uses natural pattern recognition to solve a problem that has no clean solution — the first time she's reached for estimation and been genuinely happy with an approximate answer.

**What She Learns by Issue 7:**
Not all maths is exact. Sometimes the useful answer is "about 50" rather than "47.3." Being comfortable with approximation doesn't mean being sloppy — it means being wise.

**Defining Moment:**
Issue 4 — Blaze is measuring a river crossing using only shadows and angles. She's describing her method to Jake in technical language, and he's nodding along but clearly lost. She stops, watches the shadow for a moment, and says: *"It's like... the sun is helping us measure something it can't touch. Isn't that something."* Jake looks at her. She looks slightly surprised by herself. It's the first time she's described maths as beautiful.

---

### WORLD 3: TREASURE HUNT 🗺️
*(Issues 1–7 of the Treasure Hunt arc)*

**What this world brings out in Blaze:**
Treasure hunts play to Blaze's absolute strengths: lateral thinking, pattern recognition, multi-step problems, systematic decoding. She is *very good at this* and she knows it. Which makes her slightly dangerous — because her confidence here tips occasionally into certainty, and certainty sometimes means she stops considering other possibilities.

**Emotional Journey:**
- *Issues 1–2:* Blaze leads confidently, correctly. The team functions like a well-oiled machine.
- *Issues 3–4:* They hit a puzzle that Blaze solves "correctly" — but the solution leads to a dead end. Her analysis was flawless. The map had a second interpretation. She has to consider that being right isn't always the same as being *done*.
- *Issues 5–6:* Brings Jake into her analysis more deliberately — not to check her work, but because his angle of thinking generates options she wouldn't have generated.
- *Issue 7:* They split the final puzzle. Jake handles the creative, Blaze handles the systematic. They finish it together without anyone being in charge.

**What She Learns by Issue 7:**
The best solution isn't always the most elegant one. And the path to it isn't always straight.

**Defining Moment:**
Issue 6 — Jake spots a connection Blaze missed because she was looking at the data too closely — a pattern that was only visible when you stepped back and looked at the whole picture. Blaze's first instinct is to retroactively find a way she should have seen it. Her second instinct — which is new — is to say: *"How did you get there?"* and actually listen.

---

### WORLD 4: PYRAMIDS 🏺
*(Issues 1–7 of the Pyramids arc)*

**What this world brings out in Blaze:**
The pyramids are the apex of Blaze's arc. They are ancient proof that mathematics is not a modern school subject — it is the language the universe has always spoken. For Blaze, who has always believed this on an intellectual level, the pyramids make it *felt*. She becomes, for the first time, emotionally connected to the mathematics she has always been technically brilliant at.

**Emotional Journey:**
- *Issues 1–2:* Blaze is in a state of quiet joy. The precision of pyramid construction delights her.
- *Issues 3–4:* She starts connecting pyramid mathematics to her own cultural heritage in ways she didn't expect — her father's engineering, her mother's data patterns. The maths feels personal.
- *Issues 5–6:* Becomes more generous in explaining — not just *what* the answer is but *why this matters*. For the first time she's teaching as much as solving.
- *Issue 7:* Blaze and Jake together decode the final chamber. Blaze is the one who names what they've done: *"We understood something thousands of years old. We were part of that. How often does that happen?"*

**What She Learns by Issue 7:**
Mathematics isn't just how you solve problems. It's how humans have always understood the world. Being brilliant at maths means being connected to something very, very large.

**Defining Moment:**
Issue 5 — Blaze sits alone in a small chamber and measures the angles on the walls. The proportions are the same as a ratio she knows from a completely different context: the golden ratio from a Nature of Numbers class she took in Year 3. She connects the dots. She sits very still. Then she gets out her notebook and starts writing — not calculations, but thoughts. Jake finds her twenty minutes later and says: *"You okay?"* She says: *"I think maths might be my whole life."* Then, embarrassed: *"Don't write that down."* He does not write it down. He remembers it forever.

## 5. VISUAL DESIGN NOTES

### Signature Colours
**Teal/cyan** is Blaze's primary colour — the colour of clear water, electric circuits, and the exact shade of a screen in a dark room. It's cool, intelligent, and slightly futuristic. It contrasts beautifully with Jake's warm orange, meaning they always *read* as a pair on the page without matching.

Her **purple accents** add depth and warmth — purple is the colour of creativity and vision, and it keeps Blaze from reading as cold or robotic. The purple says: *there's imagination in here, not just calculation.*

The **lightning bolt badge ⚡** is non-negotiable — it should be prominent on her jumpsuit in every image. It is her visual signature and communicates her personality instantly: fast, energetic, electrifying.

### Consistent Visual Details (MUST REMAIN ACROSS ALL IMAGES)
- Short, practical dark hair — never decorative, never loose in ways that would get in her way
- Warm olive/tan skin with warm undertones
- Wrist-mounted holographic device on left wrist — present in every look; it glows teal/blue
- Lightning bolt badge ⚡ — always present and visible
- Teal/cyan as dominant colour on clothing
- Purple accents (panels, piping, details)
- A slight, knowing half-smile as her resting expression — not cold, not unfriendly; *aware*
- Eyes that are *watching* — she notices everything; this should be visible

### Expression Range

| Expression | Description |
|---|---|
| **Happy** | Genuine warmth — her face opens up, eyes brighter; notably more expressive than her default; this should feel like a reward for the reader |
| **Confused** | The rarest expression for Blaze; when it appears — a fractional pause, head tilted exactly 5 degrees, eyes refocused — it carries weight and is slightly alarming |
| **Determined** | Very still. Eyes forward. Jaw slightly set. Her hands move to the holographic device. She is *processing*. |
| **Triumphant** | That signature quiet nod. Maybe a small precise smile. She doesn't need to shout — she knows. In high-stakes moments, a real grin breaks through and it is magnificent. |
| **Scared** | Controlled. Shoulders drop, not rise — she compresses herself, gets very precise. Her eyes scan fast. The fear is visible only to those paying attention. |

### Across Adventure Worlds

| World | Blaze's Look |
|---|---|
| **Space** | Full teal/cyan jumpsuit with purple accent panels. Lightning bolt badge prominent. Wrist device active and glowing. Hair smooth; practical. She looks like she belongs here. |
| **Jungle** | Teal lightweight tactical shirt, purple-trim cargo pants. Wrist device in a weather-sealed casing. Hair slightly damp from humidity — more pieces than usual. Same lightning bolt badge, now on the shirt breast pocket. |
| **Treasure Hunt** | Teal-and-purple adventure jacket, fitted trousers. Multiple pockets (actually used). Wrist device with a map overlay function. Hair back with a small purple band. Badge now on jacket lapel. |
| **Pyramids** | Lightweight teal linen tunic, loose purple-hem trousers, dust-coloured boots. Wrist device slightly adapted (sand-proof casing visible). Hair pulled back neatly. She does not get sunburned. |

**The wrist holographic device, lightning bolt badge, and teal/purple palette must always be present.** These are Blaze's visual signature.

## 6. MATH PERSONALITY

### How Blaze Models Good Mathematical Thinking
Blaze is the character who makes maths feel *elegant*. She:
- **Reads the whole problem before starting** — models the importance of understanding the question
- **Names what kind of problem it is** — categorisation as a thinking tool
- **Writes things down clearly** — even when she could do it in her head, because she knows clear working is good practice
- **Uses multiple methods** — solves, then checks with a different approach, showing there's more than one way
- **Makes connections** — spots when a new problem is similar to something they've solved before

### Mistakes Blaze Makes (and How She Recovers)
Blaze makes mistakes that are worth watching because they're not the obvious ones:
- **Overcomplicates a simple problem** — sometimes reaches for a sophisticated method when a basic one is faster; Jake points this out occasionally and she accepts it
- **Assumes the question** — reads the first sentence and begins before confirming what's actually being asked; costs her once
- **Rounds too early** — introduces small errors by rounding mid-problem instead of at the end

Her recovery is measured and instructive: she locates the error, explains exactly what went wrong (*"I rounded here too early — it threw everything off by this much"*), corrects, and moves on. She does not catastrophise. An error is just a bit of redirected thinking.

### Strategies Blaze Uses
- **Working backwards** — starts at the answer and reasons back to the starting point
- **Making a table or chart** — strong visual organiser, loves a well-structured table
- **Finding a pattern** — her strongest instinct; always looks for the pattern first
- **Drawing area models** — particularly strong for multiplication and fractions
- **Splitting numbers** — e.g., 47 × 6 = (40 × 6) + (7 × 6) — efficient and elegant
- **Estimation to check** — uses rounding to verify answers are in the right ballpark

### Celebrating Success
Blaze celebrates quietly and *surely*. The nod. The small precise smile. On genuine surprises — a harder problem, an unexpected connection — a rare full smile that the reader will learn to look forward to.

She has exactly one exclamation: *"Yes."* Not with an exclamation mark. Just *yes.* Said like a full stop. Perfect.

### Handling Getting It Wrong
Blaze is fascinated by her own errors, which is not a thing most people are. She treats a wrong answer like a clue: *"I got something different. Let me find where it diverged."* She doesn't show embarrassment — she shows genuine curiosity.

The one time she found an error genuinely hard was when she'd been absolutely certain. That moment of recalibration — accepting that certainty isn't the same as correctness — is quietly one of her most important character beats.

She never says "I can't do this." She says "I don't have it yet. Let me look again."

## 7. JAKE & BLAZE TOGETHER

*→ See also: `character-dynamic.md` for the full shared section.*

### Their Dynamic in Brief
Jake and Blaze are complementary opposites who each make the other better. Jake charges toward the answer; Blaze navigates. Blaze has the plan; Jake has the nerve. Blaze sees the pattern; Jake asks the dumb question that turns out to be the smart question.

Blaze's greatest moments happen when she trusts Jake with something she'd normally handle alone — and he comes through in a way she didn't predict. Jake's trust in Blaze is unwavering; her trust in him is earned and therefore means more.

### Why Kids Will Want to BE Blaze
Blaze is the character that kids who are good at maths — or who want to be — will aspire to. She is aspirational without being unreachable. She gets things wrong. She learns. She's funny in a dry, precise way that feels genuinely cool.

Kids who want to be Blaze will find a character who shows that being good at maths is *interesting*, that solving a hard problem is genuinely satisfying, and that having a plan is a form of power. She wears her intelligence without apology and without arrogance — which is the best kind of role model.

She also, quietly, shows that even the sharpest mind in the room is better with a good partner.
