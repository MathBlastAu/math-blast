# Age-Stage Development Guide

*For Math Blast | Cognitive, Linguistic & Narrative Development by Year Band*
*Australian Curriculum Years Foundation–6 | Ages 5–12*

---

## Why This Guide Matters

Writing math stories for kids is harder than it looks. A problem that's perfectly calibrated for Year 5 will confuse a Year 2 student and bore a Year 6. This guide maps the cognitive, linguistic, and motivational characteristics of each year band so content creators can pitch *exactly* right.

**Golden rule:** The story complexity, vocabulary, and narrative structure must match the year level as carefully as the math difficulty. A story about complex international space trade using multi-syllable words will fail a Year 2 reader even if the math is right.

---

## BAND 1: Foundation–Year 1 (Ages 5–6)

### Cognitive Stage
**Piagetian:** Late pre-operational → early concrete operational.
- Learn exclusively through direct physical experience and play
- Cannot hold abstract representations ("n" means nothing without a physical referent)
- Strong tendency to be confused by perceptual contradictions (a taller, thinner glass looks like it has more water)
- **Conservation** (same amount even if it looks different) is just developing — not yet reliable
- **Logical reversibility** is emerging — they can sometimes undo a physical action, but not symbolically
- Memory: short working memory (2–3 items); sequential reasoning is limited to 1–2 steps

### Mathematical Readiness (across our 4 topics)
| Topic | Foundation–Y1 Entry Level |
|-------|--------------------------|
| Fractions | Equal sharing into 2 parts (halves); "fair" as the anchor concept |
| Time | Sequence of daily events; o'clock; duration as long/short comparison |
| Equations | Physical balance; "same as"; true/false with objects |
| Division | Equal sharing of up to 20 objects; no symbolic notation |

### Vocabulary Level
- Everyday language only
- Short sentences (5–8 words typical)
- Concrete nouns (ball, cookie, box, block)
- Action verbs (share, give, count, fold, take)
- **Avoid:** fractions language (numerator, denominator), formal equation notation, multi-syllable technical terms
- **Tier 2 words to introduce gently:** equal, half, share, fair, more, less, before, after, same

### Story Complexity
- **One character** (or two with a simple interaction)
- **One problem** — single step, clear outcome
- **Familiar settings:** home, school, playground, kitchen
- **Motivational frames:** fairness ("it's not fair!"), sharing with a friend, helping someone
- **Sentence structure:** simple declarative. "Max has 8 rocks. He shares them with Izzy. How many each?"
- **Story arc:** Situation → One question → Answer revealed
- **Avoid:** time pressure, multiple characters with competing goals, villains, complex backstory

### What Engages 5–6 Year Olds
- Characters and animals they can name and care about
- Physical/tactile activities embedded in the story (fold the paper, count the dots)
- Immediate resolution — no cliff-hangers
- Silliness and warmth (aliens that eat silly foods, robots that hiccup)
- Recognition of familiar school/home patterns ("just like at lunchtime")
- **Bright colours, big fonts, plenty of white space** in magazine layout

### Sample Narrative Approach
> *"Mia the astronaut has 6 moon-berries. Her robot friend Zip is hungry too. How can Mia share them so they both get the same? Draw the berries in two groups."*

## BAND 2: Years 2–3 (Ages 7–8)

### Cognitive Stage
**Piagetian:** Concrete operational (well-established).
- Can classify, seriate (order), and understand conservation
- Logical reversibility works for physical operations
- Beginning to handle **two-step reasoning** with physical support
- Can hold 3–4 items in working memory
- Beginning symbolic thinking if anchored in concrete experience
- Strong development of **causal reasoning** ("if... then...")
- Beginning to distinguish their own perspective from others' ("theory of mind" well-established)

### Mathematical Readiness (across our 4 topics)
| Topic | Y2–Y3 Level |
|-------|-------------|
| Fractions | Halves, quarters, thirds, eighths; number line; simple set model |
| Time | Quarter-past, quarter-to, 5-minute intervals; days/weeks calendar; simple elapsed time |
| Equations | Equals as balance; one unknown (□); true/false equations; non-standard position |
| Division | Sharing AND grouping; ÷ symbol; fact families; remainders introduced |

### Vocabulary Level
- Expanding vocabulary; can handle new words if explained in context
- Sentences up to 10–12 words
- Beginning to handle subordinate clauses ("If the rocket has 12 seats and 3 rows...")
- **Tier 2 words in active use:** equal, quarter, divide, share, half, before, after, number line, pattern
- **Introducing:** fraction, denominator (label only — no formal definition yet), remainder, equation, balance

### Story Complexity
- **Two to three characters** with clear roles
- **One main problem** with 2 steps maximum
- **Mild tension:** something needs fixing, finding, or deciding
- **Settings expanding:** simple space settings work now (rocket, space station, planet surface)
- **Story arc:** Set up → Problem → Clue or tool → Solution
- **Cause-and-effect:** explicitly stated ("because," "so that," "when")
- Readers can handle **mild uncertainty** and **suspense** (will they make it in time?)
- **Villains/problems** can appear but must be non-threatening (silly, clumsy, or confused — not scary)

### What Engages 7–8 Year Olds
- **Counting, sorting, pattern-spotting** — still tactile in mindset
- Team dynamics ("let's work together")
- Mild competition (races, scores, leaderboards)
- Creatures, animals, and robots with personality
- **Secret codes and hidden messages** (even very simple ones)
- Achievement and recognition ("You solved it! Level up!")
- Real objects they can handle: calendars, clocks, fraction tiles (as story props)

### Sample Narrative Approach
> *"The space patrol has 24 fuel pods to load onto 4 ships. They need the same amount in each ship or the fleet won't fly straight. Captain Yuki and Engineer Sam argue about the best way to split them. Who do you agree with — and why?"*
*(A question with two plausible answers creates engagement and models mathematical discussion.)*

## BAND 3: Years 4–5 (Ages 9–10)

### Cognitive Stage
**Piagetian:** Solid concrete operational → emerging formal operational.
- Can perform **multi-step logical reasoning** with pictorial support
- Beginning to handle **abstract symbol manipulation** (variables, unknowns)
- Can hold and coordinate **two different representations** simultaneously (e.g., fraction + number line)
- Developing **proportional reasoning** (this is a major Year 5 achievement)
- **Metacognition** is developing — can monitor their own thinking
- Working memory: 4–5 items; sequential reasoning up to 4 steps
- **Social reasoning:** peer comparison is strong; fairness still matters but now includes complex social dynamics

### Mathematical Readiness (across our 4 topics)
| Topic | Y4–Y5 Level |
|-------|-------------|
| Fractions | Equivalent fractions; mixed numbers; comparing unlike denominators; adding/subtracting with like denominators; fractions on number line |
| Time | Elapsed time (open number line); 24-hour time; timetables; converting units |
| Equations | Variables (letters/boxes); two-step equations; inverse operations; both sides with operations |
| Division | Short division; 2–3 digit by 1 digit; area model; fraction connection; decimals introduced |

### Vocabulary Level
- Can handle longer sentences with embedded clauses
- Multi-syllable technical vocabulary accessible if used in context
- **Tier 2 active:** equivalent, elapsed, inverse, variable, remainder, quotient, duration
- **Tier 3 introducing:** denominator, numerator (now with meaning), algorithm, formula (label)
- Metalanguage emerges: "I need to first... then... because..."

### Story Complexity
- **Multi-character stories** with different goals or perspectives
- **Genuine problem** requiring 2–3 step reasoning
- **Red herrings work** — extra information the reader must ignore
- **Quest/mission structure** — characters work toward a goal with obstacles
- Settings can be fully imaginative (deep space, alien planets, time travel)
- **Character perspective** can drive engagement: "Captain Aria thinks the answer is 12. Is she right?"
- Characters can have **flaws and make mistakes** — readers can out-think a character
- **Story arc:** Setup (with embedded math data) → Obstacle → Investigation → Solution → Consequence

### What Engages 9–10 Year Olds
- **Being smarter than the characters** — solving it before the reveal
- **Codes, puzzles, and hidden patterns**
- **Competition and strategy** — "What's the BEST way to solve this?"
- **Real-world science connections** — space, nature, technology
- Emerging **identity in math**: "I'm a person who can do hard math"
- **Humour that's a bit more sophisticated** — wordplay, irony, situational humour
- Peer collaboration framing: "You and your team must decide..."
- **Complexity is rewarding**, not scary — they want to be challenged

### Sample Narrative Approach
> *"The starship's timetable shows the crew has 4 hours and 20 minutes before the meteor shower. Chief Engineer Priya has calculated they need exactly 3 hours 45 minutes to complete repairs. Navigator Kofi thinks Priya's timing is off by 30 minutes. They can't both be right. Read the mission log below and work out who has made an error — and what the correct schedule should be."*

## BAND 4: Year 6 (Ages 11–12)

### Cognitive Stage
**Piagetian:** Emerging formal operational (not fully arrived for most students).
- Can begin to reason **hypothetically** ("what if...?")
- Beginning to handle **variable thinking** (a number that could be many values)
- **Proportional reasoning** is well-developed for most
- Can deal with **counterintuitive results** if introduced carefully
- **Metacognition** is strong — can explain their own reasoning in detail
- Beginning to think about **mathematical elegance** ("is there a better way?")
- Social: strong peer identity, beginning to question authority ("prove it to me")

### Mathematical Readiness (across our 4 topics)
| Topic | Year 6 Level |
|-------|-------------|
| Fractions | Multiply/divide fractions; connect to decimals/percentages; rates and ratios; complex word problems |
| Time | Time zones; international date line; multi-step elapsed time; crossing midnight |
| Equations | Multi-step equations; variables on both sides; simple systems; construct equation from verbal description |
| Division | Long division; division of decimals; divisibility rules; division in rates/ratios; scale |

### Vocabulary Level
- Approaching adult reading level for engaged readers
- Can handle **technical registers** (science, math, engineering language)
- **Tier 3 in active use:** numerator, denominator, quotient, divisor, dividend, equation, coefficient, formula, elapsed, duration, time zone
- Can handle **conditional language:** "if... then...", "assuming that...", "given that..."
- **Metalanguage:** can discuss their mathematical thinking explicitly

### Story Complexity
- **Full story arc** — protagonist, obstacle, rising stakes, resolution
- **Ambiguity and multiple answers** are fine — "which approach is better, and why?"
- **Ethical dimensions** can be woven in: "Is it fair to round down here? Who loses out?"
- **Character-driven problems**: characters who are wrong, stubborn, or have different goals
- **Multiple data sources** in one problem (a timetable, a diagram, and a verbal clue)
- **Genre variety**: science fiction, mystery, adventure, historical — all work
- **Irony and subversion** land: a robot that makes a human mistake; a student who teaches the teacher
- **Open endings** are okay — "what do you think they should do next?"

### What Engages 11–12 Year Olds
- **Feeling respected** — problems that assume they're smart
- **Real-world stakes** — this math matters outside the magazine
- **Surprising or counterintuitive results** (dividing by a fraction makes it bigger?!)
- **Design/engineering challenges** — "design a schedule that works"
- **Debate and multiple perspectives** — "Zara and Tom get different answers. Who's right?"
- **Connection to identity**: "Math is a tool, and I can use it"
- **Pop culture resonance** without being patronising
- **Justice and fairness** in complex contexts (resource allocation, fair division)
- Self-directed problem extensions: "What happens if there are 7 people instead of 6?"

### Sample Narrative Approach
> *"Mission Chronos: The deep space relay station must synchronise its clocks with both Earth (AEST, UTC+11) and the Mars colony (operating on Earth time, UTC). A message is sent from Sydney at 14:23 AEST. The signal takes 22 minutes to reach the station. The station must relay it exactly 1 hour 15 minutes after receiving it. At what UTC time does the relay message leave the station? And what time is it in Sydney when it does? Show your reasoning."*

## Cross-Band: Narrative & Language Calibration

### Sentence Complexity by Year Band

| Band | Max sentence length | Clauses | Technical terms | Embedded data |
|------|--------------------|---------|-----------------| --------------|
| F–Y1 | 6–8 words | Simple only | Very few | Separate from story |
| Y2–Y3 | 10–15 words | 1 subordinate clause | Introduced in context | Beside the story |
| Y4–Y5 | 15–25 words | Multiple clauses | Used freely | Embedded in story |
| Y6 | 20–40 words | Complex | Technical register | Fully embedded |

### Emotional/Motivational Hooks by Year Band

| Band | Primary hook | Secondary hook | Avoid |
|------|-------------|----------------|-------|
| F–Y1 | Fairness, helping | Warmth, animals | Fear, competition, time pressure |
| Y2–Y3 | Mild mystery, teamwork | Achievement, mild competition | Complex betrayal, abstract stakes |
| Y4–Y5 | Being smarter than characters | Challenge/quest, real science | Condescension, baby problems |
| Y6 | Counterintuitive results, real stakes | Debate, design | Childish framing, lack of agency |

### Character Archetypes That Work Across All Ages
- **The Enthusiastic Newbie** (asks the questions the reader has) — all ages
- **The Confident-But-Wrong Expert** (gives the reader a chance to correct them) — Y2+
- **The Methodical Engineer** (models good mathematical thinking) — Y3+
- **The Sceptic** (demands proof, pushes deeper) — Y5+
- **The Time-Pressured Leader** (creates urgency) — all ages

## A Note on Differentiation

Math Blast may be read by students across a range of year levels. Some design strategies for inclusive use:

1. **Tiered questions on a single page:** Label questions ★ (accessible), ★★ (standard), ★★★ (extension). Same story, different demands.
2. **Visual supports always present:** Diagrams, number lines, and bar models help all students and scaffold lower years.
3. **Worked example embedded in story:** Character explains their thinking, providing a model even for struggling readers.
4. **Open-ended extension:** End each activity with "What if...?" or "Try it with different numbers" — naturally differentiates upward.
5. **Language accessibility:** Bold key math words on first use; provide a small glossary.
